SECTION 1: MODIFIED COMPARATIVE FAULT — 51% BAR — CPRC SECTION 33.001
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
Texas follows modified comparative negligence with a 51% bar. An injured person can recover damages only if they are 50% or less at fault for the incident. If the plaintiff is found more than 50% responsible (51% or greater), recovery is ZERO — complete bar.

This is fundamentally stricter than California and New York pure comparative fault systems. Insurance adjusters in Texas aggressively push fault above 50% because it eliminates payout entirely.

STATUTORY FRAMEWORK
Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 33 — Proportionate Responsibility.

CPRC 33.001: "A claimant may not recover damages if his percentage of responsibility is greater than 50 percent."

CPRC 33.012: Jury determines percentage of responsibility for each party including claimants, defendants, and settling persons.

CPRC 33.013: Court reduces recovery in proportion to claimant's responsibility when 50% or less at fault.

CPRC 33.015: Joint and several liability largely abolished for defendants 50% or less responsible — each pays only several share except for intentional torts and certain product liability.

HOW THE 51% BAR WORKS — EXAMPLES
Example 1: Total damages $200,000. Plaintiff 30% at fault. Recovery: $140,000 ($200,000 minus 30%).

Example 2: Total damages $200,000. Plaintiff 50% at fault. Recovery: $100,000 — still allowed at exactly 50%.

Example 3: Total damages $200,000. Plaintiff 51% at fault. Recovery: $0 — complete bar.

Example 4: Total damages $1,000,000. Plaintiff 49% at fault. Recovery: $510,000.

Example 5: Rear-end collision. Defendant 80%, plaintiff 20%. Specials $40,000, pain and suffering $60,000. Total $100,000. Recovery $80,000.

The cliff at 51% makes Texas comparative fault battles existential for claim value.

KEY CASE LAW
Motiva Enterprises v. Schneider Electric (2020) — Texas Supreme Court reaffirmed proportionate responsibility framework.

Federal Sign v. Texas Southern University (1995) — Comparative fault principles in premises cases.

DEFENSE TACTICS — PUSHING PAST 51%
Recorded statement within 24-48 hours hunting for admissions:
  "I didn't see him."
  "I should have been paying more attention."
  "The floor looked wet but I walked anyway."
  "I was in a hurry."
  "My phone was in my hand."

Surveillance video analysis for any moment of inattention.

Biomechanical expert arguing plaintiff's body position caused fall.

Seatbelt defense in auto cases — failure to wear seatbelt is comparative fault evidence (CPRC 33.001 allows consideration).

Pre-existing condition arguments blurring causation to shift fault.

Independent Medical Exam minimizing injury while arguing plaintiff exaggerated — affects credibility and fault allocation.

PLAINTIFF COUNTER-TACTICS
Never admit fault at scene or to adjuster.
Decline recorded statement in serious cases until counsel review.
Preserve video immediately — Texas spoliation remedies exist but prevention is critical.
Retain credible witnesses early.
Emphasize defendant's statutory violations (health codes, building codes, traffic laws).
In close liability cases, consider early mediation before defense builds 51% narrative.
Forum selection — Harris, Dallas, Travis counties vs. rural conservative venues.

VOICE ASSISTANT QUICK ANSWERS
Q: Can I sue if I was partly at fault in Texas?
A: Yes, if you are 50% or less at fault. If you are 51% or more at fault, you recover nothing in Texas.

Q: How is Texas different from California on fault?
A: California allows recovery at any fault level (pure comparative). Texas bars recovery entirely if you are more than half at fault.

SECTION 2: PREMISES LIABILITY — INVITEE, LICENSEE, TRESPASSER, OPEN AND OBVIOUS
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
Texas premises liability law distinguishes visitor status and applies different duties. Business invitees receive the highest protection. The open and obvious doctrine is a powerful defense in Texas — stronger application than in many California cases.

INVITEE — HIGHEST DUTY
Person entering property for mutual benefit of visitor and owner, often for business transaction.

Examples: Grocery store customer, retail shopper, restaurant patron, hotel guest, hospital visitor in public areas, apartment complex guest in common areas.

Duty: Property owner/possessor must use ordinary care to keep premises in reasonably safe condition. Must inspect, discover unreasonably dangerous conditions, and make safe or warn.

Elements for invitee slip-and-fall (State Dept. of Highways v. Payne, 1993):
1. Unreasonable risk of harm existed on premises.
2. Owner/possessor knew or should have known of condition.
3. Owner failed to exercise ordinary care to reduce or eliminate risk.
4. Condition proximately caused injury.

LICENSEE — INTERMEDIATE DUTY
Social guest or person entering with permission but not for commercial benefit to owner.

Examples: Friend visiting homeowner, party guest, door-to-door salesperson (sometimes classified as licensee).

Duty: Warn of dangerous conditions owner knows about that are not obvious to licensee. No general duty to inspect for unknown hazards.

TRESPASSER — MINIMAL DUTY
Person on property without permission.

Duty: No willful or wanton injury; no intentional traps. Attractive nuisance doctrine for child trespassers (Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code 75.001-75.003 — limited application).

KEY CASE — MOTEL 6 v. LOPEZ (2000)
Texas Supreme Court clarified invitee status and duty to inspect commercial premises. Business operators must take reasonable steps to discover dangerous conditions through routine inspection.

NOTICE — ACTUAL AND CONSTRUCTIVE
Actual notice: Employee saw spill, created hazard, received complaint.

Constructive notice: Hazard existed long enough that reasonable inspection would have discovered it. Plaintiff must prove duration — puddle with dried edges, track marks, surveillance timestamps.

Unlike some California mode-of-operation arguments, Texas courts require stronger notice proof in transitory substance cases.

OPEN AND OBVIOUS DOCTRINE — MAJOR TEXAS DEFENSE
If hazard is open and obvious to a reasonable person, owner may owe no duty to warn or protect.

Examples defense will argue:
  Large yellow wet floor cone visible in photo
  Bright red spilled salsa on white tile
  Obvious 6-inch step up with contrasting paint
  Parking lot pothole in daylight, clearly visible

Texas courts more willing to grant summary judgment on open and obvious than California post-Rowland analysis.

Plaintiff response: Distraction doctrine — customer looking at shelves, not floor. Unreasonable condition despite visibility. Owner created the condition.

WEATHER AND NATURAL ACCUMULATIONS
Texas ice storm cases (rare but catastrophic when they occur): Premises owner must take reasonable steps after known ice formation on walkways. "Act of God" defense if storm ongoing and remediation impossible.

Rainwater tracked into store entrance — recurring issue at Texas retail. Constructive notice if no mats, no regular mopping schedule documented.

DEFENSE FIRMS AND TACTICS
Major retail defendants use national defense counsel: Kroger, Walmart, HEB (Texas-specific grocery giant), Target.

Reservation of rights letters on commercial general liability policies.

Early 51% fault argument combined with open and obvious motion to dismiss.

Challenge medical causation with IME physicians common in Texas market.

CITY-SPECIFIC NOTES
Houston (Harris County): Massive retail footprint, humidity-related floor moisture, flooding history in parking areas. HEB dominant grocery — aggressive defense.

Dallas (Dallas County): Similar retail density, ice storm premises cases in winter events.

Austin (Travis County): Growing tech campus premises, mixed urban-rural jury pool, generally moderate plaintiff outcomes.

San Antonio (Bexar County): Military population, tourist River Walk premises liability — uneven cobblestones, bar spill cases overlap dram shop.

Rural West Texas: Conservative juries, lower verdicts, strong open and obvious sympathy for landowner defendants.

SECTION 3: STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS — CPRC 16.003, GOVERNMENT CLAIMS, DISCOVERY
================================================================================

PERSONAL INJURY — CPRC 16.003
Two years from date injury occurred to file lawsuit for tort claims including negligence, premises liability, auto accidents, dog bites (negligence theories).

Clock starts at injury date — not when treatment ends.

PROPERTY DAMAGE — CPRC 16.003
Two years for property damage claims.

WRONGFUL DEATH — CPRC 16.003
Two years from date of death.

MEDICAL MALPRACTICE — CPRC 74.251
Two years from occurrence or from date of discovery through reasonable diligence, with overall 10-year statute of repose in many scenarios. Complex — consult specialist.

TEXAS TORT CLAIMS ACT — GOVERNMENT ENTITIES
Before suing state or local government, notice requirements apply under Texas Civil Practice and Remedies Code Chapter 101.

Cities: Written notice of claim generally required within six months of incident (verify specific city charter — some require shorter periods, e.g., 90 days for certain municipalities).

State government: Notice under Chapter 101 — timing and content requirements specific.

Missing government notice can bar claim — similar urgency to California 6-month claim but Texas city-specific rules vary. Always verify charter of specific city (Houston, Dallas, San Antonio each have nuances).

MINORS
Minor's claim generally tolled — limitations begin running at 18th birthday for two-year period in many negligence cases. Verify current CPRC 16.001 for disability tolling.

DISCOVERY RULE
Limited in Texas general negligence — accrual at injury. Medical malpractice and certain fraud cases have discovery provisions.

DO NOT DELAY
Two years passes quickly. Evidence (video, witnesses) disappears in weeks. Insurer builds 51% file from day one.

SECTION 4: DOG BITE LAW — ONE-BITE RULE, LILLIAN'S LAW, LEASH ORDINANCES
================================================================================

OVERVIEW
Texas does NOT have statewide strict liability for dog bites like California Civil Code 3342. Texas follows the one-bite rule (common law) with statutory enhancements for serious attacks and local ordinance violations.

ONE-BITE RULE — GENERAL PRINCIPLE
Dog owner generally NOT liable for first bite unless owner knew or should have known dog had vicious propensities.

Knowledge proven by:
  Prior bites or attacks
  Prior aggressive behavior (growling, lunging at people)
  Complaints to owner about dog
  Breed and training evidence (fact-specific, not automatic)
  Owner statements acknowledging dog "doesn't like strangers"

Marshall v. Ranne (1974) — Foundational Texas Supreme Court case on scienter (knowledge of viciousness).

LILLIAN'S LAW — TEXAS HEALTH AND SAFETY CODE 822.005
Criminal and civil enhanced penalties for attacks by dangerous dogs causing serious bodily injury.

Owner may face felony charges if dog known dangerous and not secured.

Civilly supports liability when dog previously adjudicated dangerous or owner acted with criminal negligence.

LEASH LAW VIOLATIONS — STRICT LIABILITY PATH
Many Texas cities (Houston, Dallas, Austin, San Antonio) require leash control.

Violation of local leash ordinance can support negligence per se or strict liability theory depending on jurisdiction and ordinance language.

If dog at large in violation of ordinance bites victim — strong liability case even without prior bite if ordinance imposes strict duty.

LANDLORD LIABILITY
Landlord generally not liable for tenant's dog unless landlord had control over premises and knew of vicious dog.

Some apartment complexes have breed restrictions — violation of own rules may support negligence claim.

PRACTICAL STEPS AFTER DOG BITE IN TEXAS
Medical care immediately — rabies, infection.
Identify owner — at-large dogs may require animal control search.
Report to animal control — creates record of prior complaints if any.
Photograph injuries, dog if safe, location (fence gaps, open gate).
Witness statements.
Check city ordinance on leash and dangerous dog registry.

SETTLEMENT REALITY
First-bite cases without ordinance violation — harder to win, insurers deny or lowball.
Prior bite or at-large violation — homeowner/renter policy typically responds ($100,000-$300,000 common limits).
Severe facial scarring on child — $75,000-$300,000+ depending on liability proof.

SECTION 5: WORKERS COMPENSATION — NON-SUBSCRIBER STATUS (CRITICAL TEXAS UNIQUE)
================================================================================

TEXAS IS THE ONLY STATE WHERE MOST PRIVATE EMPLOYERS CAN OPT OUT OF WORKERS COMP
This is the single most important workers compensation fact for Texas personal injury voice assistant queries.

SUBSCRIBER EMPLOYERS (Have Workers Comp)
Exclusive remedy — employee cannot sue employer for negligence.
Benefits through Division of Workers' Compensation system:
  Medical benefits for work-related injury
  Temporary Income Benefits (TIBs) — portion of lost wages
  Impairment Income Benefits (IIBs)
  Supplemental Income Benefits (SIBs) in qualifying cases
  Lifetime Income Benefits (LIBs) for catastrophic injury
  Death benefits

Notice: Injury reported within 30 days to employer (Texas Labor Code 409.001).
Claim filing deadlines apply — consult current DWC rules.

NON-SUBSCRIBER EMPLOYERS (Opted Out — NO Workers Comp)
Employee CAN sue employer directly in negligence lawsuit for full damages including pain and suffering.

This is extraordinary compared to California mandatory comp system.

Non-subscribers lose common-law defenses in many cases:
  Cannot argue employee's negligence (comparative fault limitations under Labor Code 406.033 in employer suits)
  Cannot argue co-worker negligence as defense
  Known non-subscriber status creates jury sympathy — "they didn't even buy workers comp"

Employers must file DWC Form-005 with state indicating non-subscriber status — publicly searchable.

WHY IT MATTERS
Walmart, Target, major chains — typically subscribers.
Many Texas restaurants, small contractors, staffing companies — sometimes non-subscribers.
Construction subcontractors frequently non-subscribers — direct suit against employer possible.

PRACTICAL QUESTIONS FOR INJURED WORKER
"Does my employer have workers compensation insurance?"
Check pay stub, employee handbook, ask HR directly.
Search Texas Department of Insurance non-subscriber list.
If employer says "we have an injury benefit plan" — may be non-subscriber ERISA plan, NOT workers comp — lawsuit may still be available.

THIRD-PARTY SUITS (Both Subscriber and Non-Subscriber)
Even with workers comp, employee can sue third party who caused injury:
  Negligent driver while on delivery
  Equipment manufacturer defect
  Property owner at job site
  General contractor negligence (if employee of subcontractor)

Comp insurer has subrogation lien on third-party recovery.

RETALIATION
Texas Labor Code 451 prohibits retaliation for filing workers comp claim (subscriber employers).
Remedies include reinstatement and lost wages.
Non-subscriber context — general wrongful termination claims may apply.

VOICE ASSISTANT CRITICAL SCRIPT
Q: Can I sue my employer if I got hurt at work in Texas?
A: It depends. If your employer has workers compensation, you usually cannot sue them — you file a comp claim. But Texas allows employers to opt out of workers comp. If your employer is a non-subscriber, you may be able to sue them directly for full damages. Also, if someone other than your employer caused the injury — like another driver or a property owner — you may have a third-party lawsuit. Ask whether your employer has workers comp coverage and check the Texas non-subscriber list.

SECTION 6: MOTOR VEHICLE ACCIDENTS — 30/60/25 MINIMUMS, PIP, UM/UIM, REPORTING
================================================================================

MINIMUM LIABILITY INSURANCE — TEXAS TRANSPORTATION CODE 601.072
Texas requires minimum auto liability limits:
  $30,000 bodily injury per person
  $60,000 bodily injury per accident
  $25,000 property damage

Written as 30/60/25. High percentage of Texas drivers carry only minimums — UM/UIM essential.

PERSONAL INJURY PROTECTION (PIP)
Texas PIP is optional — insurer must offer, can be rejected in writing.
Typical PIP amounts: $2,500, $5,000, $10,000.
Covers medical bills and lost wages regardless of fault up to limits.
No mandatory no-fault system like New York — PIP is supplemental.

MEDICAL PAYMENTS (Med Pay)
Optional coverage for medical expenses — stacks with health insurance subject to coordination.

UNINSURED/UNDERINSURED MOTORIST (UM/UIM)
Optional but strongly recommended.
UM: At-fault driver has no insurance.
UIM: At-fault driver has insufficient limits (e.g., $30,000 BI but case worth $150,000).
Must be offered — rejection must be signed (Texas Insurance Code Chapter 1952).

CRASH REPORTING
TxDOT CR-2 form (Blue Form) — driver must report crash to Texas Department of Transportation within 10 days if injury or death OR property damage appears to exceed $1,000.

Police report — always call police for injury accidents. Texas Peace Officer's Crash Report (CR-3) critical for insurance.

COMPARATIVE FAULT IN AUTO — 51% BAR CRITICAL
Rear-end: Presumption against rear driver but "sudden stop" argument pushes plaintiff fault.
Intersection: Red light runner primary fault but speed of other driver allocated.
Multi-vehicle pileups: Complex fault percentages — plaintiff above 51% loses everything.

HIT AND RUN
Report immediately. UM claim with own insurer. Physical contact usually required — phantom driver claims need corroboration.

RIDESHARE
Uber/Lyft Texas operations — period-based coverage similar to other states, $1M policies during active ride.

SECTION 7: DRAM SHOP AND SOCIAL HOST LIABILITY
================================================================================

DRAM SHOP ACT — TEXAS ALCOHOLIC BEVERAGE CODE CHAPTER 2
Bars, restaurants, liquor stores may be liable for serving alcohol to obviously intoxicated person who causes injury.

Elements:
  Provider sold or served alcohol
  At time of service, person was obviously intoxicated to extent presenting clear danger
  Intoxication proximately caused plaintiff's injuries

Applies to licensed providers — not typical grocery store beer aisle unless improper service.

Social host liability: Generally NO liability in Texas for serving alcohol to adult guests in private home (statutory limitation). Exception: serving minors — Texas Alcoholic Beverage Code 106.07 — adult providing alcohol to minor may face civil liability.

COMMON SCENARIOS
Drunk driver hits plaintiff after leaving Texas bar — dram shop claim against bar PLUS driver claim.
Over-served patron fights in bar — premises liability overlap.
Wrongful death highway collision — bar served visibly intoxicated patron who drove.

EVIDENCE
Bar tab records, surveillance, witness statements from bartenders and patrons.
Blood alcohol content of driver.
Police DWI investigation.
Texas TABC violation history.

Damages can exceed driver's policy — commercial liquor liability policies often $1M+.

SECTION 8: AVERAGE SETTLEMENTS AND VALUATION IN TEXAS
================================================================================

VALUATION FRAMEWORK
Specials (medical + lost wages + property) plus pain-and-suffering multiplier.
51% bar risk — insurers lowball knowing cliff effect.
Policy limits — 30/60 auto minimums cap many recoveries at $30,000 per person from defendant.

SLIP AND FALL — TYPICAL RANGES
Minor soft tissue, full recovery: $3,000 - $20,000
Moderate with PT/chiro, 6 months: $25,000 - $75,000
MRI-documented disc, injections: $75,000 - $200,000
Surgery (shoulder, back, knee): $150,000 - $400,000
Permanent impairment: $250,000 - $800,000+
Catastrophic: $500,000 - several million (excess policies rare in retail)

AUTO ACCIDENT — TYPICAL RANGES
Minor whiplash: $4,000 - $18,000
Moderate treatment: $20,000 - $60,000
Herniated disc: $60,000 - $180,000
Surgery: $120,000 - $350,000
Wrongful death: $500,000 - $3M+ depending on county

DOG BITE — TYPICAL RANGES
With prior knowledge or ordinance violation: $25,000 - $150,000 moderate
Severe scarring child: $100,000 - $400,000
First bite no knowledge — often $0 to $15,000 if any settlement

NON-SUBSCRIBER EMPLOYER CASES
Full tort damages — pain and suffering uncapped by comp system.
Moderate back injury: $100,000 - $500,000
Severe injury: $500,000 - $5M+ depending on employer assets and insurance

REGIONAL VARIATION
Harris County (Houston): Largest verdicts in state, diverse jury pool, plaintiff-friendly relative to Texas average.
Dallas County: Strong commercial litigation bar, moderate to high awards.
Travis County (Austin): Urban moderate awards.
Rural counties: Conservative, low pain-and-suffering multipliers, strong 51% defense success.

SECTION 9: TEXAS DEPARTMENT OF INSURANCE RULES AND BAD FAITH
================================================================================

TDI REGULATES INSURANCE CLAIMS HANDLING
28 Texas Administrative Code 21.203 — Prompt payment rules.
Insurer must acknowledge claim within 15 days, begin investigation, accept or reject within 15 business days after receiving requested items OR notify why more time needed.

Texas Insurance Code Chapter 541 — Unfair settlement practices prohibited:
  Misrepresenting policy provisions
  Failing to attempt good faith settlement when liability reasonably clear
  Failing to promptly provide reasonable explanation for denial
  Unreasonable delay

Texas Insurance Code Chapter 542 — Prompt payment of claims; interest penalties for delayed payment in weather-related claims and certain other contexts — Chapter 542.060 interest on delayed claims (significant in hail cases, applies in some third-party contexts through case law).

FILE COMPLAINT: tdi.texas.gov

TOP INSURERS IN TEXAS — TACTICS
State Farm — largest market share, recorded statement day 1, 51% fault push, Colossus lowball.
Allstate — IME heavy, pre-existing degeneration arguments.
USAA — military focus, generally professional handling, still minimizes first offer.
Farmers — strong commercial premises defense.
GEICO — fast lowball, arbitration clauses, delay MRI authorization.
Progressive — Snapshot telematics for fault arguments.

APPENDIX A: HARRIS COUNTY VS. RURAL TEXAS JURY TENDENCIES
================================================================================

HARRIS COUNTY (HOUSTON)
Most populous county — diverse jury pool.
Plaintiff-friendly relative to Texas — still more conservative than California.
Strong oil, medical, construction injury dockets.
Verdicts $1M+ occur in catastrophic cases.
Corporate defendants (energy companies) sometimes draw skepticism.
Flooding and hurricane property overlap — separate from PI but insurer relationships matter.

DALLAS COUNTY
Business-oriented jury pool, significant verdict history.
Commercial litigation infrastructure — defense and plaintiff bars both sophisticated.

RURAL TEXAS (EAST, WEST, PANHANDLE)
Strong landowner sympathy.
Open and obvious defense resonates.
Low pain-and-suffering awards — $15,000 soft tissue verdicts common.
51% bar frequently dispositive — jurors willing to assign majority fault to plaintiff.
Forum non conveniens — defendants may seek to move case to rural county where accident occurred.

APPENDIX B: GROCERY STORE SLIP-AND-FALL PLAYBOOK — TEXAS EDITION
================================================================================

AT SCENE
Report to manager — HEB, Kroger, Walmart, Fiesta Mart common in Texas.
Photograph spill, lack of warning cone, footwear.
Texas stores often have Spanish-speaking staff — request incident report in your language if needed.
Humidity-related entrance moisture — document weather and lack of mats.

WITHIN 24 HOURS
Spoliation letter to corporate — HEB Grocery Company, Walmart Claims, etc.
Medical care same day — Texas insurers exploit gaps aggressively.

ADJUSTER PHASE
Expect recorded statement request — 51% fault hunting.
Decline until counsel if significant injury.

LITIGATION TRIGGERS
Denial based on no notice

Surgery required

TEXAS-SPECIFIC DEFENSES TO ANTICIPATE
No constructive notice — spill "just happened"

Open and obvious — bright floor defect

APPENDIX C: SPOLIATION AND EVIDENCE PRESERVATION IN TEXAS
================================================================================

Texas spoliation law allows adverse inference when party destroys evidence with intent or negligence after duty to preserve arose.

Send preservation letter immediately after incident to:
  Store corporate legal department
  Property management company
  Any identified third party (cleaning contractor)

Request: surveillance video, incident reports, cleaning logs, employee statements, maintenance records.

Brother v. Westco Compressors (2014) — Texas Supreme Court spoliation framework.

Failure to preserve video — jury instruction allowing inference that video would have been harmful to spoliator.

Act within 24-48 hours — many retail systems overwrite in 7-14 days.

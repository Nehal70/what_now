WHAT NOT TO SAY AFTER AN INJURY — COMPLETE GUIDE TO PROTECTING YOUR CLAIM
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

This document is the primary knowledge base reference when a user asks:
  "The store's adjuster just called me — what do I say?"
  "What should I NOT say after my accident?"
  "Can I talk to the other party's insurance company?"

Scope: United States personal injury claims — slip-and-fall, motor vehicle,
dog bite, premises liability, product liability, and related third-party claims.
Audience: Injured claimants, advocates, voice-assistant retrieval, legal assistants.
Last updated: 2026

CRITICAL RULE (READ FIRST):
  After any injury caused by someone else's negligence, every word you speak —
  to store managers, police, doctors, your own insurer, the OTHER party's
  adjuster, witnesses, family, or social media — can be used to reduce or deny
  your claim. This guide tells you exactly what NOT to say, what TO say instead,
  and how adjusters weaponize your words.

SECTION 1: THE MOST DANGEROUS PHRASES
Complete Phrase-by-Phrase Guide with Adjuster Tactics and Safer Alternatives
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Insurance adjusters are trained to extract damaging admissions from casual
conversation. The phrases below are ranked by frequency of use AND severity of
damage to your claim. Each entry includes: why it's dangerous, what the adjuster
does with it, what to say instead, and state-specific impact (especially the
51% comparative fault bar states).

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CATEGORY A: MINIMIZING YOUR INJURY
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PHRASE 1: "I'm fine."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Said at the scene, on an incident report, to a manager, or on a recorded
    statement, "I'm fine" becomes a permanent record that you were uninjured.
  - Adrenaline and shock mask pain for hours or days. Soft tissue injuries
    (whiplash, sprains, concussions) often peak 24–72 hours AFTER the incident.
  - Insurers use this phrase to argue ZERO damages or that later treatment is
    unrelated / fraudulent / exaggerated.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Quotes it in denial letters: "Claimant stated at scene she was 'fine.'"
  - Compares to later medical bills and argues malingering or secondary gain.
  - Reduces multiplier on pain-and-suffering from 3.0 to 1.0 or below.
  - Uses it in deposition to impeach you: "You told the manager you were fine,
    but now you claim severe pain — which is true?"
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - At scene: "I'm shaken up. I need to get checked out by a doctor."
  - To manager: "I'm not sure how badly I'm hurt yet. I want medical attention."
  - To adjuster: "I'm still being evaluated by my doctors. I can't characterize
    my condition until treatment is complete."
STATE-SPECIFIC IMPACT (51% BAR STATES):
  - Alabama, Arkansas, Delaware, District of Columbia, Georgia, Indiana, Iowa,
    Kansas, Louisiana, Maryland, Mississippi, Nebraska, New Mexico, North Carolina,
    North Dakota, Oklahoma, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee,
    Texas, Utah, Virginia, West Virginia, Wisconsin, Wyoming.
  - In these states, if insurer assigns you 51%+ fault AND you minimized injury,
    they may deny entirely. "I'm fine" supports BOTH lower damages AND credibility
    attacks that inflate your assigned fault ("wasn't paying attention, wasn't hurt").

PHRASE 2: "It's just a little sore / It's not that bad / I'll be okay."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Same as "I'm fine" but sounds more sympathetic — adjusters love it because
    claimants think they're being reasonable, not making a legal admission.
  - Establishes a baseline that ALL later pain must exceed to be "credible."
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Caps medical necessity: "Treatment beyond two visits is inconsistent with
    claimant's own description of minor soreness."
  - Stops authorizing MRI, PT, or specialist referrals.
  - Offers quick lowball ($500–$2,000) as "generous" given "minor" injury.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I have pain and symptoms that I'm having evaluated. I don't know the
    full extent yet."
  - Never characterize severity. Let medical records speak.

PHRASE 3: "I don't need to see a doctor."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Delayed treatment is the #1 reason insurers reduce claim value.
  - Creates gap between incident date and first medical record — insurer argues
    injury happened elsewhere or is psychosomatic.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Denies all treatment costs after "reasonable" gap (sometimes as short as 72 hours).
  - Flags claim for SIU (Special Investigations Unit) fraud review.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I'm going to get medical attention today / tomorrow."
  - Then actually go — ER, urgent care, or PCP within 24–48 hours.

PHRASE 4: "I didn't hit my head" (when you might have)
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Concussions often have delayed symptoms. Denying head impact forecloses
    TBI claims entirely.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Denies neurology, cognitive testing, concussion protocol treatment.
  - Uses incident report + your statement to block brain injury damages.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I'm not sure what I hit during the fall/collision. I want to be checked
    for head injury."
  - If asked later: "I may have struck my head — I'm following my doctor's advice."

PHRASE 5: "I'm feeling better now."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Recovery is not linear. Good days don't mean case is over.
  - Adjuster closes file, makes final low offer, argues malingering if you
    later report flare-ups.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - "Claimant stated on [date] she was feeling better — treatment from that
    point forward is unnecessary."
  - Triggers case closure and statute-running pressure tactics.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I have good days and bad days. My doctor is still treating me."
  - "I'm not fully recovered. I follow my treatment plan."

PHRASE 6: "I can still work / do everything I used to."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Directly undermines lost wages and loss of enjoyment claims.
  - Surveillance team gets authorization to follow you; one video of you
    carrying groceries ends the case.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Denies wage loss despite doctor's work restrictions.
  - Plays surveillance video in deposition or at trial.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I'm doing what I can. My employer/doctor has me on modified duty / restrictions."
  - Describe limitations, not heroics.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CATEGORY B: FAULT ADMISSIONS AND COMPARATIVE NEGLIGENCE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PHRASE 7: "It was probably my fault."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Single most case-destroying sentence in personal injury law.
  - You are doing the insurer's liability work for them — for free.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Accepts "admission against interest" — may deny claim without investigation.
  - In 51% bar states, assigns you 51%+ fault → ZERO recovery.
  - In pure comparative states, still reduces recovery by your fault percentage.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I was [doing normal activity — shopping, walking, driving with care]."
  - "There was a dangerous condition / the other driver ran the light."
  - State FACTS only. Never speculate about fault.

PHRASE 8: "I wasn't paying attention / I wasn't looking."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Classic comparative fault admission. Insurer assigns 30–70% fault to you.
  - In slip-and-fall: "customer has duty to watch where they walk."
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Pairs with "open and obvious" defense — hazard was visible, you weren't looking.
  - In Texas, Florida, and other 51% states, may bar recovery entirely.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I was walking normally through the store / driving in my lane."
  - "I did not see any warning signs or cones before the hazard."

PHRASE 9: "I should have been more careful."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Sounds humble. Legally it's negligence admission.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Transcribed as voluntary assumption of risk or contributory negligence.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "The property owner / other driver had a duty to keep the area safe."
  - Redirect to THEIR failure, not your self-blame.

PHRASE 10: "I was in a hurry / running / on my phone."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Distraction = fault. Phone use triggers subrogation from your cell carrier
    in some auto cases.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Requests cell records. Assigns significant fault percentage.
  - In rear-end cases: "claimant stopped short because distracted."
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I was moving at a normal pace for the environment."
  - Do not mention phone, even if you weren't using it.

PHRASE 11: "I knew that step was broken / the floor was wet."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Proves NOTICE to YOU — insurer argues you assumed the risk.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - "Open and obvious" + "knew of danger" = no duty to warn = no liability.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I did not notice any hazard before the incident."
  - "There were no warning signs, cones, or barriers that I saw."

PHRASE 12: "I had been drinking."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Even one drink. Insurer assigns fault, may invoke alcohol exclusion on
    defendant's policy in some scenarios, destroys credibility.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Subpoenas bar receipts, toxicology, assigns 50%+ fault.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Do not volunteer alcohol consumption. If police asked and you answered
    honestly, do not repeat to civil adjuster beyond confirming police report facts.

PHRASE 13: "I'm sorry."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Apology = admission of fault in adjuster's playbook (even if you mean
    "sorry this happened").
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - "Claimant apologized at scene — accepted responsibility."
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Express concern without fault: "Are you okay?" or say nothing about blame.
  - To adjuster: sympathy is not required. Stay factual.

PHRASE 14: "The weather was bad / it was dark."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Sounds like excuse for defendant. Actually helps THEM — "conditions were
    visible, claimant should have adjusted."
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Contributory negligence for failing to adapt to conditions.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Focus on specific defect: unmarked step, no lighting, no salt on ice
    AFTER owner knew of storm.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CATEGORY C: PRE-EXISTING CONDITIONS AND MEDICAL HISTORY
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PHRASE 15: "I've had back problems before."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Opens entire medical history for subpoena. Insurer attributes ALL pain
    to degeneration, prior injuries, aging.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Sends broad HIPAA release (5–10 years). IME doctor writes "all findings
    are pre-existing degenerative disease."
  - Reduces damages to "aggravation only" — often 10–30% of full value.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - To adjuster: "My attorney / I will provide relevant medical records through
    the proper process." (Do not discuss history on phone.)
  - To YOUR doctor: be fully honest — clinical records are protected; phone
    admissions to insurer are not.

PHRASE 16: "I have arthritis / degenerative disc disease."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Even if MRI would show this anyway, YOU saying it gives them narrative
    before records arrive.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Pre-writes denial: "Claimant admitted chronic condition predating incident."
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "My doctor is evaluating what injuries resulted from this incident."
  - Eggshell plaintiff rule: defendant takes victim as they find them (see Appendix D).

PHRASE 17: "I see a chiropractor regularly."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Insurers bias against chiro care — label claim "soft tissue fraud."
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Limits treatment authorization. Uses "prior chiro" to deny all spinal claims.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Do not volunteer treatment history to opposing adjuster.
  - Medical records will show prior care if relevant — let attorney handle.

PHRASE 18: "I was injured in a car accident a few years ago."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Invites full prior claim history search (ISO ClaimSearch database).
  - Prior claims = "serial claimant" flag even if all legitimate.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - SIU investigation. Coordinates with prior insurers to find statements
    contradicting current claim.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Do not discuss prior accidents with opposing insurer.
  - If directly asked: "I'd prefer to handle medical history through written discovery."

PHRASE 19: "I have a prior workers comp claim."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Same body part + prior WC = massive pre-existing fight.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Subpoenas WC board records, prior settlements, IME reports.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Refer to attorney. Do not elaborate by phone.

PHRASE 20: "I'm on medication for pain / anxiety / depression."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Mental health and pain meds used to argue symptom magnification, psychosomatic
    injury, or drug-seeking behavior.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Psychiatric IME. Denies emotional distress damages.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Discuss only with treating physicians. Not with opposing adjuster.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CATEGORY D: ACCEPTING OFFERS, SETTLEMENTS, AND RELEASES
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PHRASE 21: "That sounds fair."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Verbal acceptance may be used as meeting of the minds in some contexts.
  - Locks you before knowing full medical bills, future treatment, wage loss.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Sends release next day: "As we agreed on the phone..."
  - Offer is 15–30% of true value if made in first 30 days.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I need to review that in writing and understand my full medical prognosis first."
  - "I'm not prepared to discuss settlement at this time."

PHRASE 22: "I'll take it."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Early offers come BEFORE complete diagnosis (MRI, ortho, PT course).
  - Signing general release = forever waiver of ALL future claims for this incident.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Rush release with broad language covering "known and unknown injuries."
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "Please send your offer in writing. I will respond after consulting with
    my healthcare providers."

PHRASE 23: "I just want this over with."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Signals desperation. Adjuster lowers offer.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Final offer rhetoric: "This is the best we can do — you said you wanted
    to move on."
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Never reveal emotional or financial pressure to opposing insurer.

PHRASE 24: "I need money now."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Same as above. Invites predatory low offer and claims advance scams.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Quick $1,000–$3,000 "humanitarian" offer with full release.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Explore legitimate options (PIP, health insurance, disability) — not quick
    settlement with at-fault carrier.

PHRASE 25: "Okay, I won't get a lawyer."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Tells adjuster you are unrepresented and vulnerable to manipulation.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Accelerates lowball timeline. Skips fair valuation protocols.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I'm considering my options."
  - Never discuss legal representation plans with opposing adjuster.

PHRASE 26: "I signed the check."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Cashing check may equal accepting settlement if check says "full and final"
    or includes release language on back.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Sends "partial payment" check with fine print release — deposit = done.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Read EVERY check and envelope. Do not deposit without attorney review if
    injury is not fully resolved.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CATEGORY E: SPECULATION, GUESSING, AND INCONSISTENCY TRAPS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PHRASE 27: "I think I was going about 30 mph."
WHY DANGEROOUS:
  - Guess becomes sworn estimate. Black box / skid marks prove 45 → credibility gone.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Impeachment at deposition. "Inconsistent with physical evidence."
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I don't know the exact speed. I was driving appropriately for conditions."

PHRASE 28: "It happened around 2:30, I think."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Timestamp discrepancies vs. store video, receipt timestamps, cell tower data.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - "Claimant cannot reliably recall basic facts — not credible witness."
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "Approximately [timeframe]. I can confirm with my receipt / phone records."

PHRASE 29: "Maybe I tripped on my own shoelaces."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Speculation = fault admission. You don't know — don't guess.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Closes investigation: "Claimant unsure of cause — no defect established."
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I fell because of [wet floor / uneven surface / obstacle]. I'm not sure
    of every detail but the hazard was present."

PHRASE 30: "I'm not sure what happened."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Blanket uncertainty invites denial for failure to prove causation.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Denies for "lack of proof" while having store video they won't share.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - State what you DO know: location, hazard observed, immediate pain, witnesses.

PHRASE 31: "The same thing happened to my friend here last month."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Sounds helpful but without proof sounds like exaggeration.
  - IF TRUE with evidence = gold. As casual statement = "hearsay, embellishment."
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Dismisses unless you have report number. May trigger fraud flag if unfounded.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Investigate first. Provide prior incident reports through attorney if they exist.

PHRASE 32: "I told the manager [long detailed story with extras]."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Manager's report is sanitized. Your embellishments become "inconsistencies."
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Compares every version. Deposition impeachment on minor details.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Short, consistent, factual account every time. Same core facts only.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CATEGORY F: COOPERATION TRAPS AND PROCESS MISTAKES
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PHRASE 33: "Sure, I'll give a recorded statement."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - THE biggest trap. See Section 4 and Appendix A/E.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - 45–90 minute fishing expedition. Permanent record.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I'm not comfortable with a recorded statement. Please communicate in writing."

PHRASE 34: "You can have my full medical records."
WHY DANGEROOUS:
  - Broad HIPAA release lets them pull psych, substance abuse, sexual health,
    unrelated conditions. See Appendix C.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Finds any damaging history. IME confirms "pre-existing."
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I'll provide records related to this incident through appropriate legal channels."

PHRASE 35: "I don't have a lawyer and don't need one."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Green light for aggressive tactics.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Lowball, pressure, recorded statement, surveillance — full playbook.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "I'm handling this carefully and will respond in writing."

PHRASE 36: "Feel free to call me anytime."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Unrecorded follow-up calls still get note-written. Casual comments captured.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Frequent "check-in" calls to extract updates on activities, work, recovery.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - "Please email me. I'll respond when I can."

PHRASE 37: "I posted about it on Facebook."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Confirms discoverable evidence exists. They will find it.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Screenshots before you delete. Surveillance authorization.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Say nothing about social media. Lock accounts (Section 3).

PHRASE 38: "The pain is unbearable, I can't do anything ever."
WHY DANGEROUS:
  - Overstatement is as bad as minimization. One gym photo = case destroyed.
WHAT THE ADJUSTER DOES WITH IT:
  - Surveillance + IME + social media = fraud argument.
WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD:
  - Honest, measured: "I have significant pain that limits [specific activities].
    I follow my doctor's restrictions."

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
51% COMPARATIVE FAULT BAR STATES — CONSOLIDATED REFERENCE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

In these jurisdictions, if you are found MORE THAN 50% (or 51% depending on
statute) at fault, you recover NOTHING:

  Alabama, Arkansas, Delaware, District of Columbia, Georgia, Indiana, Iowa,
  Kansas, Louisiana, Maryland, Mississippi, Nebraska, New Mexico, North Carolina,
  North Dakota, Oklahoma, Rhode Island, South Carolina, South Dakota, Tennessee,
  Texas, Utah, Virginia, West Virginia, Wisconsin, Wyoming.

HOW PHRASES COMBINE TO BAR RECOVERY IN 51% STATES:
  Example (Texas slip-and-fall):
  - "I wasn't really looking" (30% fault assigned)
  - "I knew the floor gets wet there" (20% fault)
  - "I'm fine, just a little sore" (credibility attack → another 10% fault)
  - Total: 60% fault → $0 recovery despite broken wrist.

In PURE comparative states (California, New York, Florida, Illinois, etc.):
  - You can recover even at 99% fault (reduced by percentage).
  - BUT admissions still slash damages. 40% fault on $100,000 case = $60,000 loss.

MODIFIED 50% BAR (different from 51%):
  - Some states bar at 50% or greater (e.g., Colorado, Idaho in certain readings).
  - Always assume fault admissions are catastrophic.

SECTION 2: WHAT TO SAY TO EACH PARTY
Scripts, Boundaries, and Exact Language by Audience
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Different audiences have different legal relationships to you. What helps with
police may hurt with adjusters. This section provides party-by-party guidance.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2A. STORE MANAGER / PROPERTY MANAGER (AT SCENE OR SAME DAY)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

YOUR GOAL: Report incident, get medical help, preserve evidence. NOT settle,
NOT admit fault, NOT minimize injury.

SAY:
  - "I was injured on your property. I need medical attention."
  - "Please document this incident. I'd like a copy of the report."
  - "There was [wet floor / broken tile / missing handrail / ice] at [location]."
  - "My name is [name]. My phone is [number]."
  - "There were witnesses: [names if known]."
  - "I am not signing any release or settlement document today."

DO NOT SAY:
  - "I'm fine." "It was my fault." "I wasn't watching."
  - "I don't need an ambulance." (if you might)
  - "Don't worry, I won't sue." / "I won't make a big deal of this."

IF MANAGER PRESSURES YOU TO SIGN:
  - "I'm willing to complete a factual incident report with date, time, and location.
    I will not sign anything that releases your company from liability."

IF MANAGER ASKS "WHAT WERE YOU DOING?"
  - "Shopping / walking to my car / entering the building in the normal course."

IF MANAGER SAYS "WE'LL TAKE CARE OF YOUR MEDICAL BILLS":
  - "Thank you. I'll still need to seek treatment on my own and document everything."

INCIDENT REPORT TIPS: See Appendix B for field-by-field guidance.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2B. POLICE (MOTOR VEHICLE ACCIDENTS, SOME SERIOUS PREMISES CALLS)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

YOUR GOAL: Truthful factual report. Police report becomes official record.

SAY:
  - Facts: direction of travel, location, what you observed, witnesses, injuries.
  - "I am injured and need medical evaluation."
  - Point to physical evidence: skid marks, signals, store conditions.

DO NOT SAY:
  - "It was my fault" / apologies / speculation.
  - Do not minimize injury to officer.

NOTE: You must be truthful to police. Lying to police = criminal exposure.
Civil adjuster is different — you are NOT obligated to elaborate beyond police report.

IF OFFICER ASKS IF YOU'RE INJURED:
  - "Yes" or "I believe so — I need to be checked out." NOT "I'm fine."

REQUEST: Copy of police report number before scene clears.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2C. DOCTORS, ER, URGENT CARE, PARAMEDICS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

YOUR GOAL: Accurate clinical history for treatment. Medical records support claim.

SAY (FULL HONESTY HERE — records are for YOUR care):
  - How injury occurred: "I slipped on unmarked wet floor at [store]."
  - All symptoms: headache, nausea, numbness, anxiety — report everything.
  - Prior conditions when asked clinically — doctors need truth.
  - Pain level honestly. Functional limitations honestly.

DO NOT SAY:
  - Do not exaggerate (insurer sends IME; contradictions destroy credibility).
  - Do not say "I'm fine" to minimize embarrassment.
  - Do not blame yourself clinically: "I wasn't paying attention" — say what
    happened: "Floor was wet, no signs."

CRITICAL DISTINCTION:
  - Honest with DOCTOR ≠ disclose full history to ADJUSTER.
  - Doctor-patient privilege helps you. Adjuster fishing hurts you.

ASK DOCTOR TO DOCUMENT:
  - Causation language: "injury consistent with mechanism described."
  - Work restrictions in writing.
  - Follow-up plan.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2D. YOUR OWN INSURER (AUTO PIP/MEDPAY, UM/UIM, HEALTH, HOMEOWNERS)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

YOUR GOAL: Trigger coverage you pay for. Different rules than opposing insurer.

SAY:
  - Prompt notice of claim with basic facts.
  - Request PIP/medpay forms, UM claim forms if applicable.
  - Ask about coverage limits, deductibles, coordination of benefits.

CAUTION — YOUR POLICY MAY REQUIRE:
  - Cooperation clause: recorded statement, EUO (Examination Under Oath).
  - Failure to cooperate may DENY YOUR coverage (not just reduce third-party claim).
  - Still: avoid fault admissions and minimization even with own insurer.

DO NOT SAY:
  - "I don't need to file" (missed deadlines bar coverage).
  - False statements (fraud voids entire policy).

SCRIPT — CALLING YOUR AUTO INSURER AFTER ACCIDENT:
  "I'm calling to report an accident. Date: [date]. Location: [location].
  I was [rear-ended / hit by driver who ran stop sign]. I am injured and
  seeking treatment. Please open a PIP/medpay claim and send me forms.
  What are my coverage limits for medical payments and UM?"

If they request recorded statement for YOUR policy:
  - You may be contractually obligated — consult policy language.
  - Still: factual, no speculation, no fault admission, no minimization.
  - Consider brief consultation with attorney before EUO.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2E. THE OTHER PARTY'S ADJUSTER — CRITICAL SECTION
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THIS IS THE CALL USERS ASK ABOUT MOST:
  "The store's adjuster just called. What do I say?"

YOUR GOAL: Be polite, give NOTHING of value, refuse recorded statement,
communicate in writing only, do not discuss injury severity, fault, history,
settlement, or activities.

GOLDEN RULES:
  1. NO recorded statement — ever — without attorney (third-party claims).
  2. Written communication only after initial call.
  3. Confirm claim number and adjuster contact in writing.
  4. Do not discuss: fault, injury detail, pre-existing, work, social media,
     settlement, daily activities, witnesses beyond "they exist."
  5. Short call. Polite exit.

─── FIRST CONTACT SCRIPT (OPPOSING ADJUSTER) ───

ADJUSTER: "Hi, this is Mike Torres with Acme Insurance. I'm handling the
claim from the incident at Kroger on June 3rd. Is now a good time?"

YOU: "I understand you're calling about the incident. I can confirm an incident
occurred. I'm not able to discuss details over the phone right now."

ADJUSTER: "I just need a few minutes to understand what happened so we can
help you get this resolved."

YOU: "I appreciate the call. I'm not comfortable discussing this by phone.
Please communicate with me in writing. Can you email me your name, claim number,
and mailing address for correspondence?"

ADJUSTER: "We really need a recorded statement to process your claim."

YOU: "I'm not willing to provide a recorded statement. If you need information,
please send your request in writing."

ADJUSTER: "Without a statement we may not be able to pay your claim."

YOU: "I understand. Please put everything in writing. My address is [address].
Goodbye."

[END CALL — do not linger, argue, or explain]

─── IF YOU MUST SAY SOMETHING FACTUAL (WRITTEN ONLY) ───

Maximum safe written statement (email or letter):

  "On [date] at approximately [time], I was on the premises at [address].
  I sustained injuries when [one sentence: e.g., I slipped and fell on a wet
  floor in the produce aisle near register 4]. I sought medical treatment.
  I am continuing to follow up with healthcare providers. Please direct all
  communications to me in writing at [address/email]."

  DO NOT ADD: fault, prior injuries, pain level, activities, witnesses' stories,
  settlement interest, or emotional narrative.

─── EMAIL AUTO-REPLY TEMPLATE ───

  Subject: Claim [number] — Written Communication Only

  Thank you for your message regarding claim [number]. I am not available for
  phone discussions or recorded statements. Please direct all correspondence
  to this email address / [mailing address]. I will review written requests
  and respond as appropriate.

─── WHAT OPPOSING ADJUSTER WILL ASK (DO NOT ANSWER BY PHONE) ───

  - "Walk me through what happened." → WRITTEN ONLY, minimal facts.
  - "Were you on your phone?" → Decline phone discussion.
  - "How fast were you going?" → Decline.
  - "Have you had back problems?" → Decline.
  - "What did the manager say?" → Decline.
  - "Are you working?" → Decline until formal discovery.
  - "What treatment are you getting?" → They can request records properly later.
  - "What will you settle for?" → NEVER on phone.
  - "Can we send you a check for $2,000?" → Written only after full treatment.

─── IF ADJUSTER SHOWS UP IN PERSON (RARE) ───

  - Do not let them in without appointment.
  - Do not sign anything.
  - "Please contact me in writing."
  - They may be doing surveillance simultaneously — normal activity only.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2F. WITNESSES (AND WHAT TO ASK THEM NOT TO SAY)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

YOUR GOAL: Preserve neutral, factual witness accounts before insurer shapes them.

ASK WITNESS TO PROVIDE (TEXT OR EMAIL YOU WRITE DOWN):
  - What they saw: hazard, fall/collision, lighting, warnings or lack thereof.
  - Where they were standing. What they heard (splash, scream, tires).
  - Your condition immediately after (holding arm, couldn't stand, etc.).

ASK WITNESSES NOT TO:
  - Guess your speed or fault.
  - Say "she seemed fine" — they should say "she said she was hurt" or
    "she was in pain."
  - Give recorded statement to store's insurer without knowing they can decline.
  - Discuss case on social media.

SAY TO WITNESS:
  "If the insurance company calls, you can tell them what you saw. You don't
  have to give a recorded statement. Just the facts — the wet floor, no sign,
  the fall. Don't guess about whose fault it was."

NOTE: Insurer will call witnesses. You cannot control them — get written
statement from friendly witnesses EARLY (same day if possible).

SECTION 3: SOCIAL MEDIA RULES
Platforms, Surveillance, Family Tagging, and Digital Hygiene
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Insurers and defense firms routinely monitor social media within hours of claim
report. Special Investigations Units (SIU) have social media analysts. Assume
everything you post — and much that friends/family post — will be discovered.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3A. PLATFORM-SPECIFIC RULES
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

FACEBOOK:
  - Highest surveillance value. Photos, check-ins, events, "feeling better" posts.
  - Privacy settings DO NOT stop legal discovery or friend-of-friend screenshots.
  - ACTION: Deactivate or stop posting entirely. Do not delete (spoliation argument)
    without attorney advice — but stop new content.

INSTAGRAM:
  - Visual surveillance gold: gym, travel, hiking, lifting children, dancing.
  - Stories disappear but are often captured first.
  - ACTION: Private account, no new posts, no stories during claim.

TIKTOK:
  - Short videos destroy credibility instantly (dancing, workouts, pranks).
  - ACTION: No posts. Assume insurer has screen recording tools.

TWITTER / X:
  - Casual complaints about injury ("I'm fine now") used against you.
  - ACTION: No mention of accident, injury, lawsuit, or settlement.

LINKEDIN:
  - "Back to work full capacity!" posts vs. disability claim.
  - ACTION: No work-status updates during treatment.

YOUTUBE:
  - Old athletic videos resurfaced as "prior ability" even if pre-accident.
  - ACTION: Do not post; attorney may need to contextualize old content.

SNAPCHAT:
  - "Temporary" does not mean undiscoverable.
  - ACTION: Same as Instagram — no activity posts.

REDDIT / FORUMS:
  - Anonymous posts are discoverable via subpoena to platform.
  - ACTION: Do not discuss incident, injuries, insurers, or legal strategy online.

STRAVA / FITBIT / APPLE HEALTH / GARMIN:
  - Activity data subpoenaed: runs, bike rides, heart rate during "disability."
  - ACTION: Understand data exists; do not increase activity beyond restrictions;
    attorney may need to address in discovery.

GOOGLE REVIEWS / YELP:
  - Do not review the store where you fell during pending claim.

DATING APPS:
  - Profile photos and activity dates used in surveillance matrix.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3B. SURVEILLANCE — PHYSICAL AND DIGITAL
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PHYSICAL SURVEILLANCE:
  - Common in moderate-to-high value claims ($25,000+ potential).
  - Private investigators park outside home, follow to grocery store, church, PT.
  - LEGAL in most states if from public vantage point.
  - Duration: days to weeks, often triggered after recorded statement or IME.

DIGITAL SURVEILLANCE:
  - Social media monitoring (in-house or contractors like Social Intelligence).
  - Connected app data, public records, prior claim databases (ISO ClaimSearch).
  - Fake friend requests — attractive profile sends request to monitor your feed.

WHAT SURVEILLANCE LOOKS FOR:
  - You carrying groceries, playing with kids, yard work, gym, social events.
  - Inconsistency with reported restrictions — NOT proof you aren't injured,
    but jury persuasion tool.

HOW TO LIVE UNDER SURVEILLANCE:
  - Follow doctor restrictions literally.
  - If you can do light activity, ensure it's documented as approved.
  - No heroics for social media OR for neighbors' eyes.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3C. FAMILY TAGGING AND THIRD-PARTY POSTS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

YOUR POSTS ARE NOT THE ONLY RISK.

FAMILY TAGGING:
  - Spouse posts "Beach day! Kids and [injured parent] having a blast!"
  - Photo shows you holding boogie board.
  - Insurer introduces at trial. Caption is not your statement but hurts anyway.

RULES FOR FAMILY (SHARE VERBALLY):
  - Do not tag injured person in any activity photo during claim.
  - Do not mention accident, injuries, settlement, or "suing the store."
  - Set family posts to exclude injured person or delay posting until case resolves.
  - Wedding, graduation, holidays — one photo can cost tens of thousands.

FRIENDS AND COWORKERS:
  - "Welcome back!" LinkedIn endorsements contradict disability.
  - Ask close contacts not to post about your accident or recovery online.

MESSAGING APPS (TEXT, WHATSAPP, FACEBOOK MESSENGER):
  - Discoverable in litigation with subpoena.
  - Do not joke: "I'm pretending to be hurt lol" — destroys case.

EMAIL:
  - Work emails about productivity used against wage loss claims.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3D. SOCIAL MEDIA DO / DON'T CHECKLIST
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DO:
  - Tell attorney about ALL platforms you use (including old accounts).
  - Preserve existing posts (don't mass-delete after incident).
  - Increase privacy settings as baseline hygiene.
  - Ask family to stop tagging you.

DON'T:
  - Post photos of activities during treatment.
  - Post about pain level (either direction — "fine" or "worst ever").
  - Accept unknown friend requests post-accident.
  - Join "injury support" groups with public posts about your case.
  - Respond to insurer's "we saw your Facebook" with explanations by phone.

SECTION 4: FIRST 48-HOUR ADJUSTER CALL — FULL DIALOGUE SCRIPTS
Multiple Scenarios with Word-for-Word Responses
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Timeline: Store/property GL claims typically trigger adjuster contact within
24–48 business hours. Auto claims often faster (same day). This section models
real adjuster openings and pressure tactics with safe responses.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCENARIO 1: STANDARD OPENING — "FRIENDLY" ADJUSTER
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONTEXT: Slip-and-fall at grocery store. 36 hours post-incident. You went to ER.
Adjuster has incident report but no recorded statement.

[RING]

YOU: Hello?

ADJUSTER: Hi, may I speak with [Your Name]?

YOU: This is [Name].

ADJUSTER: Hi [Name], my name is Jennifer Walsh. I'm a claims adjuster with
Hartford Commercial Insurance. I'm calling about the incident at FreshMart on
Oak Street this past Tuesday. First, I want to say I'm really sorry you were
hurt. How are you feeling?

YOU: [TRAP — do not answer how you're feeling] I understand you're calling
about the incident. I can confirm something occurred. I'm not able to discuss
details on the phone.

ADJUSTER: I completely understand. I'm just trying to get your side of the story.
The store reported that you slipped near the floral department. Can you tell me
what happened from your perspective? It'll only take a few minutes.

YOU: I'd prefer to handle this in writing. Can you send me your contact
information and claim number by email?

ADJUSTER: Sure, I can do that. Before I let you go — were there any wet floor
signs out that you noticed?

YOU: I'm not discussing details by phone. Please email me.

ADJUSTER: Okay. Is [your address on file] still correct?

YOU: Please send everything in writing and I'll confirm.

ADJUSTER: We'll be in touch. Feel free to call me back anytime.

YOU: Thank you. Goodbye.

[Hang up. Do not call back.]

POST-CALL:
  - Document: date, time, adjuster name, insurer, phone number, what was said.
  - If email arrives, reply with written-communication-only template (Section 2E).

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCENARIO 2: RECORDED STATEMENT PRESSURE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONTEXT: Rear-end collision. At-fault driver's insurer. Adjuster insists
recording is "required."

ADJUSTER: This is David Chen with Progressive, claim number 24-8847291. I need
to take your recorded statement to open the bodily injury portion of your claim.
It's standard procedure — takes about 15 minutes. Is now okay?

YOU: I'm not willing to give a recorded statement.

ADJUSTER: I hear you, but we can't evaluate your claim without it. It's in your
best interest to get this done quickly so we can get you a payment.

YOU: Please put your requests in writing. I'm not providing a recorded statement.

ADJUSTER: If you refuse, we may have to close your claim without payment. I don't
want that to happen to you.

YOU: I understand what you're saying. Communicate with me in writing at [email].
Goodbye.

ADJUSTER (if persists): Can I at least ask — were you injured?

YOU: [Optional minimal response] I sought medical care. I won't discuss further
by phone. Goodbye.

KEY: Threat to deny is pressure tactic. Third-party claims proceed via records,
demand letters, and litigation without your recorded statement.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCENARIO 3: "OFFER EXPIRES" / QUICK CASH PRESSURE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONTEXT: 48 hours post soft-tissue MVA. Adjuster offers $1,500, says must
accept today.

ADJUSTER: We've reviewed the police report. Liability is clear. I'd like to offer
you $1,500 to cover your medical bills and close this out quickly. I can send
the release today if you accept. This offer expires Friday.

YOU: I'm not prepared to discuss settlement. I'm still treating. Send anything
in writing and I'll review when my medical course is clearer.

ADJUSTER: This is a fair offer given that you're probably just sore. These offers
don't stay open — we'd have to re-evaluate if you hire a lawyer.

YOU: I understand. Please email me your offer in writing. I won't accept anything
by phone. Goodbye.

DO NOT SAY: "That's too low" (reveals you're negotiating). "I might get a lawyer"
(gives them strategy). "I need more" (engagement).

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCENARIO 4: SYMPATHETIC ADJUSTER — EMOTIONAL RAPPORT BUILDING
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CONTEXT: Elderly claimant, fall at big-box store. Adjuster uses warmth and
personal connection.

ADJUSTER: Mrs. Johnson, I've been doing this job for twelve years and I hate
seeing people hurt at stores. My mom had a fall last year — I know how scary
it is. I want to make sure you're taken care of. Can you walk me through what
happened? I promise I'm on your side.

YOU: Thank you for calling. I'm not comfortable discussing the incident by phone.
Please send me your information in writing.

ADJUSTER: I understand you're cautious. That's smart. But if I can't get your
story, the company might think you're hiding something. I'm trying to protect
you here.

YOU: I appreciate that. Written communication works best for me. What's your
email address?

ADJUSTER: Before we switch to email — you said you were fine when the manager
helped you up, right?

YOU: [TRAP — do not confirm or deny manager's version] I'm not discussing details
by phone. Please email me. Goodbye.

NOTE: Sympathy is trained Phase 1 of recorded statement playbook (see insurance_claims.txt).

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCENARIO 5: ADJUSTER ALREADY HAS INCIDENT REPORT — CONTRADICTION FISHING
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ADJUSTER: I have the store report here. It says you were running through the
aisle and declined medical help. Is that accurate?

YOU: I haven't seen the store report. I'm not giving a statement by phone.

ADJUSTER: So you dispute that you were running?

YOU: I'm not discussing this by phone. Send your requests in writing.

[Do not say "I wasn't running" — that's still a statement. Written only later.]

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCENARIO 6: THEY SAY CALL IS NOT RECORDED (IT STILL COUNTS)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ADJUSTER: This isn't a recorded statement — just a friendly conversation so I
can note your account.

YOU: I'd still prefer written communication only.

NOTE: Adjuster notes are transcribed into claim file and used in deposition.
"Not recorded" ≠ "not used against you."

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SCENARIO 7: VOICEMAIL — WHAT TO LEAVE IF YOU MUST CALL BACK
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

If returning call only to get claim number:

  "This is [Name], date of birth [DOB if needed for ID]. I'm returning your
  call regarding an incident on [date]. Please email me at [email] with your
  name, claim number, and mailing address. I am not providing a recorded
  statement by phone. Thank you."

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
48-HOUR MASTER CHECKLIST (ADJUSTER CONTACT PERIOD)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

□ Medical treatment initiated (ER/urgent care/PCP within 24–48 hours)
□ Incident reported to property owner in writing if possible
□ Photos of scene/hazard/injuries preserved
□ Witness names and statements collected
□ Pain journal started
□ Social media locked down — no new posts
□ Opposing adjuster: refused recorded statement, requested writing
□ Own insurer notified if coverage applies
□ Call documented: who, when, what they said
□ No settlement discussion by phone
□ No HIPAA release signed
□ Shoes/clothing from incident preserved
□ Attorney consultation scheduled if injury beyond minor bruise

SECTION 5: DOCUMENTATION THAT WINS CASES
Ranked Evidence List with How to Obtain Each Item
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Strong documentation offsets bad luck with adjusters. Ranked by impact on
settlement value and trial success.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
TIER 1 — CASE-MAKING EVIDENCE (HIGHEST IMPACT)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

RANK 1: PHOTOGRAPHS / VIDEO OF HAZARD AT SCENE (SAME DAY)
  Why: Proves notice, dangerous condition, lack of warning.
  How: Phone photos/video immediately. Wide shot + close-up. Multiple angles.
  If missed same day: return ASAP; store may fix hazard — still photograph.
  Subpoena store security video within days (attorney sends preservation letter).

RANK 2: MEDICAL RECORDS FROM FIRST 24–48 HOURS
  Why: Causation link, objective injury documentation.
  How: ER discharge papers, urgent care summary, ambulance run sheet.
  Request complete records 30 days later — early records may omit detail.

RANK 3: INCIDENT REPORT (YOUR COPY OR YOUR WRITTEN ACCOUNT)
  Why: Establishes date, time, location, notice to defendant.
  How: Ask manager at scene. If refused, email store same day documenting report.
  See Appendix B for fields to complete.

RANK 4: WITNESS STATEMENTS (WRITTEN, SIGNED, DATED)
  Why: Independent corroboration beats he-said-she-said.
  How: Witness texts/email you facts same day. Notarized statement if possible.
  Attorney affidavits later.

RANK 5: SECURITY / SURVEILLANCE FOOTAGE
  Why: Often shows hazard, fall mechanics, no warning signs, employee mopping.
  How: Spoliation letter immediately (attorney). FOIA if public entity.
  Store systems auto-delete 7–30 days — SPEED MATTERS.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
TIER 2 — DAMAGES AND CREDIBILITY EVIDENCE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

RANK 6: CONTINUOUS MEDICAL TREATMENT RECORDS (NO GAPS)
  How: Treat consistently per doctor orders. Document why any gap (insurance delay).

RANK 7: LOST WAGE DOCUMENTATION
  How: Employer letter on letterhead: dates missed, rate, hours. Pay stubs,
  tax returns if self-employed. FMLA paperwork.

RANK 8: PAIN JOURNAL (DATED ENTRIES)
  How: Daily or weekly: pain 1–10, sleep, activities missed, medications.
  Authentic contemporaneous notes admissible in some jurisdictions.

RANK 9: PHYSICAL EVIDENCE — FOOTWEAR, CLOTHING
  How: Bag unwashed shoes/clothes worn at fall. Wet floor = wet shoes.

RANK 10: PRIOR COMPLAINTS ABOUT SAME HAZARD
  How: FOIA for public property. Discovery for commercial. OSHA records workplace.
  Other victims — police reports, online reviews mentioning same defect.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
TIER 3 — SUPPORTING AND REBUTTAL EVIDENCE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

RANK 11: POLICE REPORT (MVA)
  How: Request from department or DMV portal. 5–10 business days typical.

RANK 12: WEATHER / LIGHTING DATA
  How: NOAA historical weather, timestamped photos showing poor lighting.

RANK 13: BUILDING CODE / OSHA VIOLATIONS
  How: Inspector reports, code enforcement complaints, expert inspection.

RANK 14: DEFECTIVE PRODUCT RECORDS
  How: Preserve product, receipt, recall databases (CPSC.gov).

RANK 15: COMMUNICATIONS LOG WITH INSURERS
  How: Save all letters, emails. Note all calls (date, adjuster, summary).

RANK 16: EXPERT REPORTS (BIOMECHANICAL, SAFETY, ECONOMIC)
  How: Retained through attorney for larger cases.

RANK 17: TAX RETURNS / FINANCIAL RECORDS (SELF-EMPLOYED WAGE LOSS)
  How: 2–3 years pre-incident for baseline earnings.

RANK 18: LIFE CARE PLAN (CATASTROPHIC INJURY)
  How: Medical expert projection of future care costs.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
HOW TO OBTAIN RECORDS — PRACTICAL GUIDE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

MEDICAL RECORDS (YOUR PROVIDERS):
  - HIPAA right of access: request in writing from each facility.
  - Allow 30 days. May charge reasonable copying fee.
  - Request: "Complete records including imaging, nursing notes, PT notes."

OPPONENT'S INSURER RECORDS:
  - They obtain via authorization or subpoena after lawsuit filed.
  - Do NOT sign broad authorization voluntarily (Appendix C).

EMPLOYMENT RECORDS:
  - You: pay stubs, employer letter.
  - Opponent subpoenas employer in litigation for full wage history.

STORE VIDEO:
  - Attorney spoliation letter to store and insurer within 72 hours.
  - Letter demands preservation of all video, logs, incident reports, mop records.

GOVERNMENT RECORDS:
  - FOIA request to city/county for sidewalk, transit, school incidents.

ISO CLAIMSEARCH:
  - You cannot access directly. Attorney discovers prior claims in litigation.

APPENDIX A: ADJUSTER LEADING QUESTIONS AND HOW TO DEFLECT
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Leading questions are designed for "yes" answers that become admissions.
If trapped on phone (shouldn't happen), use deflection — but BEST answer is
"I don't discuss this by phone."

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FAULT / ATTENTION QUESTIONS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: "You weren't looking where you were walking, correct?"
DEFLECT: "I'm not giving a statement by phone."
IF FORCED IN WRITING: "I was walking in the store in the normal course of shopping."

Q: "You would agree the floor was wet because it had been raining, right?"
DEFLECT: Written only. "There was water on the floor. I did not see warning signs."

Q: "You could have gone around the spill, couldn't you?"
DEFLECT: "I'm not admitting fault. The hazard was present without adequate warning."

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
INJURY MINIMIZATION QUESTIONS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: "When you left the store, you walked out on your own, didn't you?"
DEFLECT: "I sought medical evaluation. I won't discuss details by phone."

Q: "You're feeling a lot better now, aren't you?"
DEFLECT: "I'm still under medical care."

Q: "It was just a bruise, right?"
DEFLECT: "My doctors are documenting my injuries."

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PRE-EXISTING QUESTIONS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: "You've treated for back pain before this incident, haven't you?"
DEFLECT: "Send written requests for any records you're entitled to through proper channels."

Q: "Your MRI shows degenerative changes — that means old injury, correct?"
DEFLECT: [Writing only, with attorney] Do not agree with medical conclusions from adjuster.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ACTIVITY / SURVEILLANCE QUESTIONS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: "You went to your kid's soccer game last Saturday?"
DEFLECT: "I won't discuss my activities by phone."

Q: "You haven't missed any work, have you?"
DEFLECT: "Employment information will be provided through appropriate process."

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
SETTLEMENT QUESTIONS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: "What number would make this go away?"
DEFLECT: "I'm not discussing settlement at this time."

Q: "If we offered $5,000, would you take it?"
DEFLECT: "Send any offer in writing."

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
TRAP: TWO-CHOICE QUESTIONS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: "Were you running or walking fast through the aisle?"
DEFLECT: "I was shopping normally." (Reject the false binary.)
Never pick one harmful option to be cooperative.

APPENDIX B: INCIDENT REPORT — FIELD-BY-FIELD GUIDE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Store incident reports are written by employees for their insurer. If YOU complete
fields or sign manager's version, every word matters.

FIELD: DATE AND TIME
  Enter: Exact from receipt, phone timestamp, or witness.
  Why: Video preservation depends on correct timestamp.

FIELD: LOCATION ON PREMISES
  Enter: Specific: "Aisle 7 produce, between registers 3–4, near floral."
  Why: Vague location loses video and witness correlation.

FIELD: DESCRIPTION OF INCIDENT
  SAFE: "I slipped and fell on wet floor" / "I tripped on uneven mat."
  UNSAFE: "I wasn't paying attention" / "I should have seen it."
  Keep one sentence. Factual mechanism only.

FIELD: WITNESSES
  Enter: Full names, phones if known. "Several customers observed — names unknown."
  Why: Triggers insurer witness calls — also your future evidence.

FIELD: INJURIES
  SAFE: "Pain in wrist, hip, head — seeking medical evaluation."
  UNSAFE: "None" / "Minor" / "Fine."

FIELD: MEDICAL TREATMENT SOUGHT
  SAFE: "Yes — going to ER / urgent care today."
  UNSAFE: "No" / "Declined."

FIELD: EMPLOYEE STATEMENTS
  If manager writes "customer said she was fine" — DO NOT SIGN without correction.
  Request amendment: "Customer reported pain and intent to seek medical care."

FIELD: PHOTOS TAKEN BY STORE
  Note: "I also took photos." Request copies (often refused — take your own).

FIELD: RELEASE / LIABILITY WAIVER
  NEVER SIGN at scene. If pressured: "I will not sign a release."

FIELD: YOUR SIGNATURE
  Signing factual incident report ≠ signing release. Read entire document.
  If ANY release language: do not sign.

AFTER INCIDENT REPORT:
  Email yourself copy of what you submitted. Photo of form if allowed.
  If store refuses copy: document refusal with witness name.

APPENDIX C: HIPAA RELEASE TRAPS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Adjusters send HIPAA authorization forms early. Signing wrong form = case damage.

THE TRAP:
  Form authorizes ALL records from ALL providers for 5–10 years, including:
  - Psychiatric treatment
  - Substance abuse (42 CFR Part 2 may apply separately)
  - HIV/status, reproductive health
  - Unrelated ER visits
  - Prior injuries to unrelated body parts

WHY THEY WANT IT:
  - Find any damaging history before offering money.
  - IME doctor reviews full history, attributes pain to pre-existing.
  - No litigation needed — you volunteered records.

SAFE PRACTICES:
  - Do not sign at scene, over phone, or with first adjuster letter.
  - Narrow authorization (attorney-drafted): specific providers, date range
    from incident forward, injury-related visits only.
  - Psych records: highly protected — do not authorize unless litigation requires.

IF ADJUSTER SAYS: "We can't pay without HIPAA release."
  RESPONSE: "Send your request in writing. I will provide records related to
  this incident through appropriate channels."

POST-LITIGATION:
  Discovery still gets relevant records — but scope is fought by your attorney,
  not unlimited voluntary dump.

WORKERS COMP CROSSOVER:
  WC records especially toxic in third-party claims — prior same-body-part injury.
  Attorney handles coordination.

APPENDIX D: THE "EGGSHELL PLAINTIFF" RULE EXPLAINED
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LEGAL PRINCIPLE:
  Defendant takes the victim as they find them. If you have osteoporosis,
  prior fusion, brittle bones, or chronic conditions, defendant is still liable
  for ALL damages flowing from their negligence — including exaggerated harm
  due to your vulnerability.

PLAIN LANGUAGE:
  Thin skull, eggshell spine, pre-existing degeneration — negligent party does
  not get a discount because you were easier to hurt.

EXAMPLE:
  Minor rear-end impact causes herniation in claimant with prior asymptomatic
  disc disease. Surgery required. Defendant pays full surgery damages despite
  "pre-existing" MRI findings — because negligence caused symptomatic injury.

WHY ADJUSTERS STILL USE PRE-EXISTING ARGUMENT:
  - Most claimants don't know eggshell rule.
  - Many settle cheap before attorney cites case law.
  - IME doctors write "all symptoms pre-existing" knowing jury may disagree.

WHAT YOU SHOULD DO:
  - Be honest with YOUR doctors about prior conditions.
  - Do NOT admit to adjuster that "it's all old injury."
  - Let attorney argue eggshell / aggravation doctrine.

KEY CASE NAMES (for attorney reference, varies by state):
  - Eggshell skull doctrine
  - Crumbling skull (defense variant — only liable for difference)
  - Aggravation of pre-existing condition vs. new injury

DO NOT SAY TO ADJUSTER:
  "I already had a bad back so this isn't their fault."
INSTEAD:
  "My doctor is treating injuries from this incident."

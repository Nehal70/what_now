SECTION 1: WHAT IS A PERSONAL INJURY CLAIM?
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

A personal injury claim is a legal demand for monetary compensation from a person,
business, or entity whose wrongful conduct caused physical, emotional, or financial
harm to another. Most personal injury cases are civil tort actions based on
NEGLIGENCE — failure to exercise reasonable care.

COMMON PERSONAL INJURY CASE TYPES:
- Motor vehicle accidents (car, truck, motorcycle, pedestrian, bicycle)
- Slip-and-fall / trip-and-fall (premises liability)
- Dog bites and animal attacks
- Workplace injuries (workers compensation — separate system — plus third-party tort)
- Medical malpractice
- Product liability (defective products)
- Nursing home abuse and neglect
- Wrongful death (survival and wrongful death statutes)
- Assault and battery (when insurance coverage available)
- Negligent security (inadequate property security leading to assault)

TWO PATHS TO RESOLUTION:
1. INSURANCE CLAIM / PRE-SUIT NEGOTIATION — demand letter, adjuster negotiation,
   mediation, settlement agreement
2. LAWSUIT — complaint filed in civil court, discovery, trial or settlement during litigation

Most cases settle without trial. Trial is backup when insurer denies liability or
lowballs damages beyond reasonable negotiation range.

KEY DISTINCTION — INSURANCE CLAIM vs. LAWSUIT:
Reporting claim to insurer starts administrative process. Filing lawsuit is legal
action subject to statute of limitations. Negotiating with adjuster does NOT stop
SOL clock in any state unless rare written tolling agreement.

SECTION 2: THE FOUR ELEMENTS OF NEGLIGENCE — DUTY, BREACH, CAUSATION, DAMAGES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

To win a negligence personal injury case, plaintiff must prove ALL FOUR elements
by a preponderance of the evidence (more likely true than not — 51%+ certainty).

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ELEMENT 1: DUTY OF CARE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEFINITION:
Legal obligation to conform to a standard of reasonable conduct to avoid foreseeable
harm to others.

WHO OWES DUTY TO WHOM — EXAMPLES:

DRIVER TO OTHER ROAD USERS:
Every motorist owes duty to drive reasonably — obey traffic laws, maintain attention,
control vehicle. Extends to pedestrians, cyclists, passengers.

STORE OWNER TO CUSTOMERS (INVITEES):
Business open to public owes duty to maintain safe premises, inspect for hazards,
warn of or fix dangerous conditions. Applies to Walmart, restaurants, hotels, offices.

HOMEOWNER TO SOCIAL GUESTS:
Homeowner owes duty of reasonable care to invited guests — fix known hazards or warn.
Lesser duty to undiscovered trespassers; higher in some states for child trespassers
(attractive nuisance doctrine).

LANDLORD TO TENANT:
Landlord owes duty to maintain common areas, respond to habitability hazards, secure
known security risks in many jurisdictions.

DOCTOR TO PATIENT:
Healthcare provider owes professional standard of care — same as reasonably prudent
physician in same specialty under similar circumstances.

MANUFACTURER TO CONSUMER:
Product maker owes duty to design and manufacture safe products with adequate warnings.

NO DUTY SITUATIONS (examples):
- No general duty to rescue strangers (with exceptions)
- No duty to protect against open and obvious hazards in many states
- No duty to control third persons except special relationships (landlord-tenant,
  employer-employee in limited contexts)

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ELEMENT 2: BREACH OF DUTY
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEFINITION:
Defendant failed to meet the standard of care — acted unreasonably or failed to act
when reasonable person would have acted.

BREACH = UNREASONABLE CONDUCT under the circumstances.

EXAMPLES BY CASE TYPE:

AUTO ACCIDENT BREACH:
- Running red light
- Texting while driving (negligence per se if statute violated)
- Following too closely, rear-end collision
- Driving under influence
- Failing to yield at crosswalk

SLIP-AND-FALL BREACH:
- Employee mopped floor, no warning cone placed
- Known broken step not repaired for months
- Inadequate lighting in stairwell where fall occurred
- Failure to inspect premises on reasonable schedule
- Ice accumulation from owner's drainage design (unnatural accumulation)

DOG BITE BREACH (in non-strict-liability states):
- Owner knew dog was aggressive, failed to restrain
- Violation of leash ordinance (negligence per se)
Note: Strict liability states (FL, IL, CA, etc.) may not require separate breach proof

WORKPLACE THIRD-PARTY BREACH:
- Equipment manufacturer failed to guard dangerous machinery
- General contractor failed to enforce safety standards at construction site

NEGLIGENCE PER SE:
Violation of safety statute/regulation that causes the type of harm the law was
designed to prevent = automatic breach (still must prove causation and damages).
Example: Building code violation — missing handrail on stairs — tenant falls.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ELEMENT 3: CAUSATION (Actual Cause + Proximate Cause)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

CAUSATION has two components in most jurisdictions:

ACTUAL CAUSE (cause-in-fact):
"But-for" test: But for defendant's breach, would injury have occurred?
Example: But for store leaving spill unattended 45 minutes, customer would not
have slipped and fractured wrist. Causation satisfied.

Alternative tests in complex cases: substantial factor test when multiple causes.

PROXIMATE CAUSE (legal cause):
Was injury a foreseeable result of defendant's breach? Not too remote?
Example: Driver runs red light, hits car, victim breaks leg — foreseeable.
Example (often breaks proximate cause): Driver runs red light, victim in crash has
heart attack from stress unrelated to physical trauma — disputed proximate cause.

INTERVENING CAUSES:
Subsequent events that break chain of causation may shield original wrongdoer.
Superseding intervening cause (intentional criminal act of third party) may cut
liability — unless defendant's negligence made harm foreseeable (negligent security).

MEDICAL CAUSATION:
Physician testimony often required linking accident to injury diagnosis.
Pre-existing conditions complicate but do not eliminate causation (see eggshell
plaintiff rule, Section 8).

COMPARATIVE FAULT INTERACTION:
If plaintiff also negligent, causation of damages apportioned by fault percentages.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ELEMENT 4: DAMAGES
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

DEFINITION:
Quantifiable harm resulting from defendant's breach and causation.

Without damages, no compensation — even if duty, breach, and causation proven.

ECONOMIC (SPECIAL) DAMAGES — dollar amounts documented:
- Past medical expenses (ER, surgery, PT, prescriptions, future medical if proven)
- Lost wages (time missed from work)
- Loss of earning capacity (future income reduction from permanent impairment)
- Property damage (vehicle repair, destroyed personal property)
- Out-of-pocket costs (medical equipment, transportation to treatment)

NON-ECONOMIC (GENERAL) DAMAGES — pain and suffering:
- Physical pain
- Emotional distress, anxiety, PTSD
- Loss of enjoyment of life
- Disfigurement and scarring
- Loss of consortium (spouse's claim for loss of companionship)
- Permanent disability and impairment

PUNITIVE DAMAGES (rare in negligence):
Punish egregious conduct (drunk driving, intentional harm). Not available against
government entities in most jurisdictions. Capped in many states.

EXAMPLE — FULL ELEMENTS WALKTHROUGH (Grocery Store Fall):

DUTY: Store owed customer reasonable care to maintain safe shopping floor.
BREACH: Store employee created wet floor, failed to cone or clean for 40 minutes
  (proven by video). Breached duty of reasonable care.
CAUSATION: But-for wet floor, customer would not have fallen. Foreseeable that
  unattended spill causes slip injury. Orthopedist links fall to fractured hip.
DAMAGES: $85,000 medical (surgery, rehab), $12,000 lost wages, $150,000 pain/suffering
  for permanent limp and reduced mobility. Total: $247,000.

If jury finds customer 20% at fault (comparative fault state): net recovery $197,600.

SECTION 3: COMPARATIVE FAULT SYSTEMS — COMPLETE 50-STATE CLASSIFICATION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

When more than one party contributes to an accident, fault is apportioned. Your
state's fault rule determines whether you can recover at all and how much.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CATEGORY A: PURE COMPARATIVE NEGLIGENCE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

RULE: Plaintiff can recover damages reduced by fault percentage EVEN IF plaintiff
is 99% at fault. No complete bar for partial fault alone.

FORMULA: Recovery = Total Damages × (100% − Plaintiff Fault %)

1.  Alaska
2.  Arizona
3.  California
4.  Kentucky
5.  Louisiana
6.  Mississippi
7.  Missouri
8.  New Mexico
9.  New York
10. Rhode Island
11. Washington
12. South Dakota (pure comparative for personal injury — verify current statute)
13. Florida — NOTE: Florida CHANGED to 51% bar effective March 24, 2023. Florida
    is NO LONGER pure comparative. Listed here for historical context only.

ACTIVE PURE COMPARATIVE STATES (12, excluding Florida):
Alaska, Arizona, California, Kentucky, Louisiana, Mississippi, Missouri, New Mexico,
New York, Rhode Island, Washington, South Dakota

EXAMPLE (California):
Damages $100,000. Plaintiff 80% at fault. Recovery: $20,000.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CATEGORY B: MODIFIED COMPARATIVE NEGLIGENCE — 51% BAR (GREATER THAN 50%)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

RULE: Plaintiff recoverable ONLY IF fault is 50% OR LESS. If plaintiff fault is
51% or greater → COMPLETE BAR — zero recovery.

FORMULA: If fault ≤ 50%: Recovery = Total Damages × (100% − Fault %)
         If fault > 50%: Recovery = $0

EXAMPLE (Texas / Illinois / Florida):
Damages $500,000. Plaintiff 51% at fault. Recovery: $0.
Damages $500,000. Plaintiff 50% at fault. Recovery: $250,000.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CATEGORY C: MODIFIED COMPARATIVE NEGLIGENCE — 50% BAR (50% OR MORE)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

RULE: Plaintiff barred if fault is 50% OR GREATER. Must be 49% or less to recover.
Stricter than 51% bar — being equally at fault bars recovery entirely.

FORMULA: If fault < 50%: Recovery = Total Damages × (100% − Fault %)
         If fault ≥ 50%: Recovery = $0

    in some readings — treat as 51% bar state

CORRECTION — WEST VIRGINIA: 51% bar (greater than 50%). Remove from 50% list.

EXAMPLE (Colorado):
Damages $200,000. Plaintiff 50% at fault. Recovery: $0 (50% bar).
Damages $200,000. Plaintiff 49% at fault. Recovery: $102,000.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CATEGORY D: PURE CONTRIBUTORY NEGLIGENCE (COMPLETE BAR FOR ANY FAULT)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

RULE: If plaintiff is even 1% at fault, plaintiff recovers NOTHING. Harshest system.
Exceptions: "Last clear chance" doctrine may rescue claim if defendant had final
opportunity to avoid harm. Some states apply modified contributory in specific contexts.

STATES (5 + DC):
1.  Alabama
2.  District of Columbia
3.  Maryland
4.  North Carolina
5.  Virginia

EXAMPLE (Virginia):
Damages $1,000,000 catastrophic injury. Plaintiff 5% at fault (slightly speeding).
Recovery: $0 under pure contributory negligence.

PRACTICAL IMPACT:
Insurers in contributory negligence states aggressively argue ANY plaintiff fault.
Recorded statements extraordinarily dangerous — "I should have been more careful"
may destroy case entirely.

SECTION 4: STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS — ALL 50 STATES REFERENCE TABLE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Missing the statute of limitations (SOL) = permanent bar to filing lawsuit.
Insurance negotiation does NOT extend SOL unless rare written agreement.

GENERAL RULE: SOL runs from DATE OF INJURY (accident date) for bodily injury.
Exceptions: discovery rule (malpractice, latent injury), minor tolling, government
notice requirements (MUCH shorter than SOL).

GOVERNMENT CLAIMS: Separate and shorter NOTICE periods often required BEFORE lawsuit.
See Section 10. Always check government rules independently of general SOL.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
OTHER COMMON LIMITATIONS PERIODS (GENERAL GUIDANCE)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Wrongful death: Often 2-3 years from death date (state-specific)
Medical malpractice: Often 2-3 years with discovery rule and outer limits

Intentional torts (assault): Sometimes longer than negligence SOL

MINOR TOLLING:
Most states toll SOL until injured child reaches age 18, then period runs.
Do NOT rely on tolling without attorney — varies by claim type and state.

SECTION 5: UNIVERSAL POST-INJURY CHECKLIST
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Organized by timeframe. Applies to virtually all personal injury scenarios nationwide.
Adapt for specific state rules (PIP 14-day Florida, Illinois 45-day workers comp,
government notice periods).

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
IMMEDIATELY — AT SCENE / FIRST HOUR
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

□ SAFETY FIRST — move to safe location if possible; do not leave scene of traffic
  accident before police arrive (unless medical emergency requires transport)

□ CALL 911 if injuries, significant property damage, hit-and-run, or hostile party

□ EXCHANGE INFORMATION (auto accidents):
  - Names, phone numbers, addresses
  - Driver's license numbers
  - Insurance company and policy numbers
  - Vehicle make, model, license plate
  - Do NOT discuss fault or apologize

□ PHOTOGRAPH / VIDEO:
  - All vehicles, damage angles, license plates
  - Scene overview — intersection, traffic signals, road conditions
  - Hazard causing fall (spill, broken step, ice patch, pothole)
  - Lack of warning signs or cones
  - Your visible injuries
  - Footwear and clothing (premises cases — insurers attack shoe choice)

□ WITNESSES:
  - Full names and phone numbers of anyone who saw incident
  - Brief statement on video if willing ("I saw the shelf collapse")

□ REPORT TO AUTHORITY:
  - Police report for auto accidents
  - Store/property manager for premises — insist incident report be created
  - Supervisor/HR for workplace — verbal then written immediately after

□ DO NOT:
  - Admit fault ("I'm sorry," "I wasn't looking")
  - Sign anything from at-fault party or their insurer
  - Give recorded statement to at-fault insurer at scene
  - Post about accident on social media
  - Leave without reporting to property manager (premises)

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SAME DAY — WITHIN 24 HOURS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

□ SEEK MEDICAL ATTENTION — even if feeling "okay"
  Adrenaline masks pain. Delayed treatment = insurer reduces value.
  Florida auto: MUST treat within 14 days for PIP — start day 1.

□ NOTIFY YOUR INSURER (auto accidents) — UM/UIM, MedPay, PIP require prompt notice

□ OBTAIN INCIDENT REPORT NUMBER from store, police, or employer

□ SEND SPOLIATION / PRESERVATION LETTER to property owner demanding preservation of:
  - Surveillance video (critical — many systems overwrite in 7-30 days)
  - Incident reports and sweep logs
  - Maintenance records
  - Employee statements
  Template: certified mail + email to store manager, corporate legal, insurer

□ START INJURY JOURNAL — date, pain level 1-10, activities you couldn't do,
  sleep disruption, medications taken

□ DOCUMENT LOST WAGE impact — notify employer of injury-related absence

□ CONSULT ATTORney (free consultation) if:
  - Hospital visit required
  - Broken bone, head injury, laceration needing stitches
  - Government property involved
  - Serious dog bite
  - You were told incident was "your fault"

□ DOG BITE: Report to Animal Control same day — rabies status, quarantine, record

□ WORKPLACE: Complete employer accident report IN WRITING — Illinois 45-day rule,
  most states require "prompt" reporting

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
WITHIN ONE WEEK (DAYS 2-7)
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

□ FOLLOW UP MEDICAL CARE — primary care, orthopedist, PT as directed
  Gaps in treatment used to argue injury not serious or healed

□ REQUEST POLICE REPORT COPY (usually available 3-7 business days)

□ CONFIRM VIDEO PRESERVATION — call store legal/corporate confirming spoliation
  letter received; document response

□ GATHER DOCUMENTATION:
  - All medical bills and explanation of benefits (EOBs)
  - Prescription receipts
  - Mileage log to medical appointments
  - Pay stubs showing lost wages

□ IDENTIFY ALL INSURANCE POLICIES that may apply:
  - At-fault party auto/homeowners/commercial GL
  - Your auto UM/UIM/MedPay
  - Your homeowners/renters if relevant
  - Umbrella policies

□ GOVERNMENT ENTITY: If city bus, public sidewalk, school, transit — FILE NOTICE
  immediately (many states: 6 months to 1 year, some shorter)

□ AVOID INSURER RECORDED STATEMENT until consulting counsel (serious cases)

□ SOCIAL MEDIA: Set profiles private; do not post physical activities inconsistent
  with injury; do not accept unknown friend requests

□ PHOTOS: Continue documenting bruising progression, swelling, scarring

═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
ONGOING — WEEKS THROUGH RESOLUTION
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

□ ATTEND ALL MEDICAL APPOINTMENTS — rescheduled missed appointments documented

□ TREAT TO MMI (Maximum Medical Improvement) before settling — settling early
  forecloses future surgery, permanent impairment claims

□ CALENDAR SOL DEADLINE on day one — set reminders at 18 months, 21 months, 23 months

□ RESPOND TO INSURER DOCUMENT REQUESTS narrowly — not 10-year full medical history

□ DO NOT SIGN:
  - Broad HIPAA authorization (all providers all time)
  - Release of all claims for partial payment
  - Settlement without understanding lien obligations

□ TRACK ALL EXPENSES in spreadsheet or app

□ COMMUNICATE WITH ATTORNEY if represented before giving any statement

□ INDEPENDENT MEDICAL EXAM (IME) by insurer doctor:
  - Attend if required by policy/state law or court order
  - Bring friend as witness; document length and thoroughness of exam
  - Do not minimize symptoms; be honest and consistent with medical records

□ MEDIATION: Prepare narrative, organize exhibits, know bottom-line settlement number

□ LIENS: Resolve health insurance, Medicare, Medicaid, workers comp liens before
  accepting net settlement figure

□ SETTLEMENT: Ensure release scope limited to this incident only; confirm all parties
  and insurers included; get payment timeline in writing (typically 14-30 days)

SECTION 6: WHEN YOU NEED A LAWYER vs. WHEN YOU MAY HANDLE IT YOURSELF
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Most personal injury attorneys offer FREE initial consultations and work on
CONTINGENCY (no fee unless recovery — see Section 7). When in doubt, consult free.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CASES YOU MAY HANDLE WITHOUT ATTORNEY (SMALL / CLEAR / LOW EXPOSURE)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Characteristics:
- Minor soft tissue injury ( soreness resolving in days)
- Full recovery within 4-6 weeks, no MRI findings
- Clear liability (rear-ended at stoplight, admitted fault)
- Medical bills under $3,000-$5,000
- No lost wages or minimal (1-2 days)
- At-fault party has insurance with adequate limits
- Insurer offering fair settlement covering all specials + modest pain/suffering
- No dispute over fault percentage
- No government entity involved
- No workers comp overlap

Process:
Submit demand with bills and records → negotiate with adjuster → signed release → payment.
Calculate pain/suffering multiplier (typically 1.5x-3x medical specials for minor cases).

Risks of DIY:
- Signing overbroad release or HIPAA authorization
- Missing SOL or government notice deadline
- Accepting lowball without knowing fair value
- Overlooking UM/UIM claim on own policy
- Unresolved liens taking entire settlement

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
CASES WHERE YOU SHOULD HIRE AN ATTORNEY (STRONGLY RECOMMENDED)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

INJURY SEVERITY TRIGGERS:
□ Broken bone or fracture
□ Surgery recommended or performed
□ Hospital admission or ER with significant treatment
□ Head injury, concussion, TBI symptoms
□ Permanent scarring or disfigurement
□ Symptoms lasting 3+ months without resolution
□ Permanent impairment rating from physician
□ Wrongful death of family member

LIABILITY TRIGGERS:
□ Fault disputed — insurer blaming you
□ Contributory negligence state (AL, DC, MD, NC, VA) — any fault argument dangerous
□ 51% or 50% bar state with disputed facts
□ Multiple at-fault parties (multi-car pileup, construction site)
□ Hit-and-run with UM claim complexity
□ Premises case requiring proof of notice (768.0755 Florida, etc.)

INSURANCE TRIGGERS:
□ At-fault party uninsured or underinsured
□ Commercial policy / trucking company / rideshare
□ Policy limits may not cover damages (catastrophic injury)
□ Bad faith conduct by own insurer (UM denial)

PROCEDURAL TRIGGERS:
□ Government entity involved (city, county, state, school, transit)
□ Workers comp + third-party overlap
□ Medical malpractice
□ Product liability
□ Nursing home abuse
□ SOL under 6 months remaining

INSURER BEHAVIOR TRIGGERS:
□ Recorded statement demanded before any offer
□ Surveillance observed
□ IME scheduled early
□ Full medical history demanded going back 10+ years
□ Denial letter received
□ Offer before treatment complete

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
DECISION MATRIX — QUICK SCORING
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Score 1 point for each YES:
[ ] Medical bills exceed $5,000
[ ] Lost wages exceed 1 week
[ ] Injury still symptomatic after 30 days
[ ] Fault disputed by any party
[ ] Government property or vehicle involved
[ ] Insurer has not accepted liability in writing
[ ] You are in contributory negligence state
[ ] Pre-existing condition being used against you
[ ] Catastrophic or permanent injury suspected

0-1 points: May self-handle with caution; free consult still wise
2-4 points: Strongly recommend attorney
5+ points: Hire attorney immediately — case too complex for DIY

SECTION 7: CONTINGENCY FEES AND LEGAL COSTS EXPLAINED
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

STANDARD CONTINGENCY FEE STRUCTURE:
Personal injury attorneys typically charge 33⅓% (one-third) of gross recovery if
case settles before lawsuit filed, and 40% if lawsuit filed and resolved before trial.
Trial or appeal may increase to 40-45% depending on contract and jurisdiction.

NO RECOVERY = NO FEE:
If case loses at trial or no settlement obtained, client owes NO attorney fee.
Exception: Client may still owe case costs (see below) depending on fee agreement.

WHAT CONTINGENCY FEE COVERS:
- Attorney time (investigation, negotiation, litigation)
- Paralegal and staff time
- Legal strategy and valuation advice
- Correspondence with insurer and opposing counsel

CASE COSTS (separate from fee — may be deducted from recovery):
- Court filing fees ($200-$500 typical)
- Service of process on defendants
- Medical record retrieval ($15-$100+ per provider)
- Expert witness fees ($3,000-$15,000+ for orthopedist, economist, accident reconstruction)
- Deposition court reporter costs
- Mediation fees ($500-$1,500 split between parties)
- Trial exhibits and technology

COST REIMBURSEMENT CLAUSE:
Most contingency contracts provide attorney advances costs during case. At settlement:
Costs deducted from client's share AFTER or BEFORE fee calculation depending on contract.
READ FEE AGREEMENT — "costs off the top" vs. "costs from client's share after fee"

EXAMPLE SETTLEMENT MATH:
Gross settlement: $100,000
Case costs advanced: $5,000
Attorney fee 33%: $33,000
Client net: $100,000 - $5,000 costs - $33,000 fee = $62,000
(Liens for health insurance subrogation may further reduce net)

LIEN NEGOTIATION:
Experienced PI attorneys negotiate health insurer liens down (often ERISA plans
require negotiation expertise). Attorney value often exceeds fee through lien reduction alone.

HOURLY vs. CONTINGENCY:
Some attorneys offer hourly for small cases — rare in PI. Contingency aligns attorney
incentive with client recovery.

STATUTORY FEE CAPS:
Some states cap attorney fees in specific case types (medical malpractice — Florida
has sliding scale; some workers comp fee schedules). Standard negligence PI generally
uncapped but must be reasonable.

WHAT TO ASK AT FREE CONSULTATION:
1. What percentage fee before and after filing suit?
2. Who advances costs? What happens to costs if we lose?
3. Will you negotiate health insurance liens?
4. Who is my primary contact — attorney or paralegal?
5. What is your experience with my injury/case type?
6. What is realistic settlement range?
7. Is lawsuit necessary or can this settle pre-suit?


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SECTION 8: THE EGGSHELL PLAINTIFF RULE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LEGAL DOCTRINE:
Defendant takes plaintiff as they find them. If plaintiff has pre-existing physical
condition, fragility, or susceptibility to injury, defendant is liable for ALL damages
flowing from the accident — even if a healthy person would have suffered less harm.

LATIN ROOT: "Eggshell skull" — person with fragile skull hit; minor blow causes
severe injury. Defendant liable for full harm despite unforeseeable severity.

APPLICATION:
- Elderly plaintiff fractures hip in low-impact fall — full damages despite fragility
- Plaintiff with degenerative disc disease suffers herniation from minor rear-end — liable
  for exacerbation and full treatment
- Osteoporosis victim suffers worse fracture — defendant pays full fracture damages
- Mental health pre-existing condition worsened by trauma — damages for aggravation

WHAT DEFENDANT CANNOT DO:
- Escape liability because plaintiff was "already injured" or "already weak"
- Reduce damages to what "average healthy person" would have suffered

WHAT DEFENDANT CAN DO:
- Argue accident did NOT cause new injury — only coincidental timing
- Introduce pre-accident medical records to distinguish aggravation vs. new injury
- Argue comparative fault if plaintiff's own negligence contributed
- IME doctor opines injury unrelated to accident (counter with treating physician)

PRACTICAL IMPACT ON CLAIMS:
Insurers ALWAYS request full medical history searching for pre-existing conditions.
Pre-existing condition on SAME body part does NOT kill case — eggshell rule protects
aggravation damages. Different body part pre-existing generally irrelevant.

DOCUMENTATION STRATEGY:
- Treating physician letter distinguishing pre-accident baseline from post-accident change
- MRI comparison if pre-accident imaging exists
- Journal documenting functional decline after accident vs. before

VOICE ASSISTANT SCRIPT:
"They found a pre-existing condition in my records. Can I still recover?"
"The eggshell plaintiff rule says the defendant is responsible for all harm they caused,
even if you were more susceptible to injury because of a prior condition. You can recover
for the worsening of a pre-existing injury if the accident aggravated it. You cannot recover
for conditions unrelated to the accident."


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SECTION 9: SPOLIATION OF EVIDENCE — PRESERVATION AND CONSEQUENCES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

DEFINITION:
Spoliation is destruction, alteration, or failure to preserve evidence relevant to
reasonably foreseeable litigation.

WHY IT MATTERS IN PI:
Surveillance video overwritten in 30 days. Sweep logs discarded. Defective product
destroyed. Spoliation can destroy plaintiff's ability to prove notice or liability.

TYPES OF EVIDENCE COMMONLY SPOILATED:
- Store surveillance CCTV (7-30 day auto-delete common)
- Body cam / dash cam footage
- Vehicle black box (EDR) data after total loss
- Defective staircase or product repaired before inspection
- Employee statements not documented
- Maintenance logs rotated out

SPOLIATION LETTER (PRESERVATION DEMAND):
Send within 24-48 hours of incident to:
- Property owner / store manager
- Corporate registered agent / legal department
- Insurance company of at-fault party
- Property management company

Letter should demand preservation of:
- All video from 2 hours before to 2 hours after incident
- Incident reports, OSHA logs, sweep logs
- Employee schedules and names of staff on duty
- Maintenance and repair records for hazard area
- Photographs taken by employees

Send via certified mail AND email. Keep delivery confirmation.

LEGAL CONSEQUENCES OF SPOLIATION (vary by state):
- Adverse inference instruction: jury told destroyed evidence would have been unfavorable
  to spoliator
- Sanctions: monetary penalties, attorney fees
- Dismissal of defendant's answer or default on liability (extreme cases)
- Separate tort claim for spoliation in some states (Florida recognizes spoliation remedies)
- Criminal obstruction charges (rare)

DEFENDANT DEFENSES TO SPOLIATION CLAIMS:
- No duty to preserve until litigation reasonably anticipated
- Evidence destroyed in ordinary course of business before notice
- Evidence not relevant
- Plaintiff delayed unreasonably in sending preservation letter

PLAINTIFF BEST PRACTICES:
- Send spoliation letter same day or next day
- Follow up confirming receipt
- File lawsuit early if defendant unresponsive and SOL permits
- Document your own evidence immediately (photos, witness info) — don't rely on defendant

SECTION 10: GOVERNMENT CLAIMS OVERVIEW — FEDERAL, STATE, LOCAL
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Suing government is harder than suing private parties. Sovereign immunity limits
who can be sued, for how much, and on what timeline. MISSING ADMINISTRATIVE DEADLINES
bars case even if general SOL has not expired.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
FEDERAL GOVERNMENT — FEDERAL TORT CLAIMS ACT (FTCA)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

STATUTE: 28 U.S.C. 1346(b), 28 U.S.C. 2671-2680

PROCESS:
1. File Standard Form 95 (SF-95) administrative claim with appropriate federal agency
2. Agency has 6 months to respond (approve, deny, or let lapse)
3. If denied or no response after 6 months, file lawsuit in U.S. District Court

DEADLINE: SF-95 must be filed within 2 YEARS of accrual of claim.

EXAMPLES:
- Slip at U.S. Post Office
- VA hospital medical malpractice (separate rules may apply)
- National park trail injury
- Federal vehicle accident

EXCLUSIONS: Intentional torts by federal employees, combat activities, many discretionary
function immunity issues.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
STATE GOVERNMENT — VARIES BY STATE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Each state has own sovereign immunity waiver statute.

Florida: Fla. Stat. 768.28 — notice to agency, 3-year limit, $200K/$300K caps
California: Government Claims Act — 6-month claim to state/local before suit
Texas: Texas Tort Claims Act — notice within 6 months for city claims in many cases
New York: Notice of Claim within 90 days for municipal defendants
Illinois: Court of Claims for state; 745 ILCS 10/ for local — 1-year notice

COMMON THEMES:
- Written notice required before lawsuit
- Shorter notice period than general SOL (60 days to 1 year common)
- Damage caps ($100K-$500K typical without legislative claim bill)
- No punitive damages against government
- Discretionary function immunity for policy decisions

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
LOCAL GOVERNMENT — CITIES, COUNTIES, SCHOOL DISTRICTS, TRANSIT
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Most procedurally unforgiving category for pro se claimants.

EXAMPLES OF SHORT DEADLINES:
Illinois local: 1 year written notice (745 ILCS 10/8-101)
California local: 6 months (Gov Code 911.2)
New York municipal: 90 days Notice of Claim (General Municipal Law 50-e)
CTA Chicago: 6 months for some claims (705 ILCS 25/)

NOTICE CONTENT typically requires:
- Date, time, exact location
- Detailed factual narrative
- Injury description and damages
- Witness names

FAILURE TO FILE NOTICE = case dismissed with prejudice in most jurisdictions.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
PRACTICAL GOVERNMENT CLAIMS CHECKLIST
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

□ Identify EXACT entity (City of X vs. County of Y vs. State DOT — suing wrong
  entity wastes critical time)
□ Research notice deadline IMMEDIATELY — day one
□ File notice via method statute requires (certified mail to clerk, specific form)
□ Keep proof of delivery
□ Wait for denial or statutory period before filing suit
□ Respect damage caps in settlement expectations
□ Hire attorney experienced in government tort claims — procedural traps abundant

SECTION 11: MEDIATION IN PERSONAL INJURY CASES
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

DEFINITION:
Mediation is voluntary (usually) confidential settlement conference facilitated by
neutral third party — the mediator. Not binding unless agreement signed.

WHEN MEDIATION OCCURS:
- Pre-suit voluntary mediation (parties agree before filing)
- Court-ordered mediation after lawsuit filed (mandatory in many jurisdictions before trial)
- Workers comp mediation (separate administrative process)

PARTICIPANTS:
- Plaintiff and plaintiff's attorney
- Defendant(s) and defense attorney
- Insurance adjuster with authority to settle (or home office on phone for large cases)
- Mediator (often retired judge or experienced PI attorney)

PROCESS:
1. Opening session — mediator explains rules; each side may give brief statement
2. Caucus — mediator separates parties into rooms, shuttles offers/demands
3. Negotiation rounds — demand starts high, offer starts low, converge
4. Agreement or impasse — if agreement, signed memorandum of understanding same day;
   formal settlement documents follow

CONFIDENTIALITY:
Statements in mediation generally inadmissible at trial if case doesn't settle.
Allows frank discussion without prejudicing trial position.

MEDIATOR ROLE:
Facilitate negotiation, reality-check both sides, identify obstacles, suggest creative
solutions (structured settlement, medical payment plan). Mediator does NOT decide case.

PREPARATION FOR PLAINTIFF:
- Organize demand packet: medical records, bills, photos, lost wage proof
- Know bottom-line number (minimum acceptable settlement)
- Understand lien impact on net recovery
- Dress professionally — defense may observe you
- Be patient — mediation can take 4-8 hours
- Emotional preparation — first offer will feel insulting; normal

TYPICAL MEDIATION OUTCOMES:
- 70-80% of mediated PI cases settle at mediation
- Impasse leads to continued litigation or later settlement closer to trial
- Partial settlement possible in multi-defendant cases

COSTS:
Mediator fee $300-$1,500+ per session, often split 50/50 between parties
Attorney time billed against recovery per contingency agreement

ADVANTAGES:
- Faster than trial (months vs. years)
- Certainty vs. jury unpredictability
- Privacy — no public trial record
- Control over outcome — trial puts decision in jury hands

DISADVANTAGES:
- May settle for less than trial value if pressure to avoid trial risk
- Bad faith low offers at mediation frustrate plaintiffs
- Requires both sides willing to negotiate in good faith


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SECTION 12: TYPES OF DAMAGES — ECONOMIC AND NON-ECONOMIC
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

ECONOMIC DAMAGES (specials — documented with bills and records):
- Past and future medical expenses
- Past and future lost wages
- Loss of earning capacity (vocational expert may prove)
- Property damage
- Household services ( hiring help for tasks you cannot perform)
- Out-of-pocket expenses related to injury

NON-ECONOMIC DAMAGES (general — no fixed price tag):
- Pain and suffering (past and future)
- Emotional distress
- Loss of enjoyment of life
- Disfigurement and humiliation from scarring
- Loss of consortium (spouse's separate claim in most states)

VALUATION METHODS:
- Multiplier method: Economic specials × 1.5 to 5+ depending on severity
- Per diem method: Daily pain value × days of suffering
- Comparable verdict research in jurisdiction
- Life care plan for catastrophic injury (future medical costing)

DAMAGE CAPS BY STATE (selected):
- Medical malpractice caps in many states (CA MICRA $250K non-economic pain/suffering)
- Punitive damage caps widespread
- General negligence PI: most states no cap (Texas non-economic cap only in medical malpractice)


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SECTION 13: COMMON DEFENSES INSURERS RAISE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

1. COMPARATIVE / CONTRIBUTORY FAULT — you caused or contributed to accident
2. PRE-EXISTING CONDITION — injury existed before accident (counter: eggshell rule)
3. FAILURE TO MITIGATE — you didn't treat promptly or follow doctor orders
4. LACK OF NOTICE (premises) — owner didn't know and couldn't know of hazard
5. OPEN AND OBVIOUS — hazard was visible; no duty to protect
6. ASSUMPTION OF RISK — you voluntarily accepted known danger
7. INCONSISTENT STATEMENTS — recorded statement differs from medical records
8. GAPS IN TREATMENT — weeks between appointments means not really injured
9. IME DISPUTE — insurer doctor says no injury or unrelated to accident
10. POLICY EXCLUSIONS — intentional acts, business use exclusion on auto, breed exclusion
11. STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS — filed too late
12. FAILURE TO PRESERVE EVIDENCE — no photos, no witnesses, no proof


═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
SECTION 14: SETTLEMENT FUNDAMENTALS AND TIMELINE
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

PHASE 1 — TREATMENT (weeks to 18+ months):
Do not settle until MMI. Insurer pressure to settle early = undervaluation.

PHASE 2 — DEMAND (after MMI):
Attorney or claimant sends demand letter with records, bills, wage loss, narrative.

PHASE 3 — NEGOTIATION (30-120 days):
Adjuster responds with offer. Counteroffers exchanged. 3-5 rounds typical.

PHASE 4 — LAWSUIT (if no resolution):
Complaint filed before SOL. Discovery: interrogatories, depositions, document requests.
6-18 months typical before trial setting.

PHASE 5 — MEDIATION / TRIAL:
Mediation often court-ordered. Trial if no settlement — jury verdict or bench decision.

SETTLEMENT DOCUMENTS:
- Release of all claims (read scope carefully)
- Confidentiality clause (sometimes negotiable)
- Lien resolution confirmation
- Payment within 14-30 days typical

AVERAGE TIMELINES (rough):
Minor soft tissue pre-suit: 3-6 months from accident
Moderate injury with PT: 6-12 months
Surgery cases: 12-24 months
Litigated cases: 18-36+ months
Government claims: add administrative notice period at front

APPENDIX A: VOICE ASSISTANT QUICK-ANSWER SCRIPTS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Q: "What do I need to prove in a personal injury case?"
A: "You need four things: duty of care, breach of that duty, causation linking the
breach to your injury, and damages — medical bills, lost wages, pain and suffering.
All four must be proven."

Q: "Can I recover if I was partially at fault?"
A: "It depends on your state. In pure comparative states like California and New York,
you recover reduced by your fault percentage. In 51% bar states like Texas, Illinois,
and Florida, you're barred if more than 50% at fault. In Alabama, Maryland, North
Carolina, and Virginia, any fault at all can bar recovery completely."

Q: "How long do I have to file a lawsuit?"
A: "Every state is different — from 1 year in Tennessee, Kentucky, and Louisiana to
6 years in Maine and North Dakota. Most states are 2 or 3 years. The clock usually
starts on the date you were injured. Government claims often have much shorter notice
requirements."

Q: "What should I do right after an injury?"
A: "Get medical care, photograph the scene and your injuries, report the incident,
get witness names, don't admit fault, don't give a recorded statement to the at-fault
insurer, and send a letter demanding preservation of surveillance video within 24 hours."

Q: "Do I need a lawyer?"
A: "For minor injuries with clear liability and low medical bills, you may handle it
yourself. For broken bones, surgery, disputed fault, government involvement, or bills
over five thousand dollars, get a free consultation with a personal injury attorney.
They work on contingency — no fee unless you recover."

Q: "What is the eggshell plaintiff rule?"
A: "The defendant must take you as they find you. If you had a pre-existing condition
or fragility that made your injuries worse, they're still liable for all the harm
they caused — they can't escape by saying a healthier person would have been hurt less."

Q: "What is spoliation?"
A: "When someone destroys or fails to preserve evidence like surveillance video.
Send a preservation letter immediately after your injury demanding they save all video
and records. If they destroy evidence after your letter, courts can punish them with
sanctions or tell the jury the evidence would have hurt their case."

APPENDIX B: GLOSSARY OF KEY TERMS
═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Adjuster — Insurance company representative who evaluates and negotiates claims
Contingency fee — Attorney paid percentage of recovery only if case wins
Demand letter — Written settlement request with supporting documentation
Discovery — Pre-trial exchange of evidence in lawsuit
Duty — Legal obligation to act reasonably
Economic damages — Quantifiable financial losses (bills, wages)
Eggshell plaintiff — Victim's pre-existing fragility doesn't reduce defendant liability
General damages — Non-economic pain and suffering compensation
IME — Independent Medical Exam by doctor chosen by insurer
Liability — Legal responsibility for harm caused
Lien — Third party's legal claim on settlement (health insurer, Medicare, Medicaid)
MMI — Maximum Medical Improvement; treatment plateau
Negligence — Failure to exercise reasonable care causing harm

Subrogation — Insurer's right to recover payments from at-fault party or settlement
Tort — Civil wrong causing harm, remedied by damages
UM/UIM — Uninsured/Underinsured motorist coverage on your auto policy
Verdict — Jury or judge's decision at trial
Wrongful death — Claim by survivors for death caused by negligence
